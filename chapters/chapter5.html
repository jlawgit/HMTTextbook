Chapter 5

Heat Transfer in Polymer Extrusion

The extruder barrel is a multi-zone heat exchanger wrapped around a screw. Heat flows simultaneously from barrel heaters into the polymer melt and from the hot melt outward through the barrel wall to cooling jackets. The concepts of thermal resistance from Chapters 2 and 3 do all the work here, applied to the geometry and materials of real processing equipment.

✍
Cold Recall — Before You Read

Close your notes. Try each question from memory, then reveal the answer. The ones you cannot recall are exactly what this chapter reinforces.

RC-1. Write Fourier's law for steady 1-D conduction through a flat wall. Define every symbol and give its units.

$$q'' = -k\frac{dT}{dx} \quad \text{or for a wall of thickness } L\text{:} \quad \dot{Q} = kA\frac{\Delta T}{L}$$ where $k$ = thermal conductivity (W/mK), $A$ = area (m²), $\Delta T$ = temperature difference across the wall (K or °C), $L$ = thickness (m). The minus sign shows heat flows from hot to cold. The conductive resistance is $R_{\text{cond}} = L/(kA)$.

RC-2. Two flat walls in series: wall 1 has resistance $R_1$ and wall 2 has resistance $R_2$. Write the total resistance and the heat flow rate if the outer temperatures are $T_A$ and $T_B$.

$$R_{\text{total}} = R_1 + R_2, \qquad \dot{Q} = \frac{T_A - T_B}{R_{\text{total}}}$$ This is the thermal circuit analogy. Each layer adds its resistance. The largest resistance controls the heat flow.

RC-3. What is the convective resistance $R_{\text{conv}}$? What physical quantity does Newton's law connect?

$$R_{\text{conv}} = \frac{1}{hA}$$ Newton's law of cooling: $\dot{Q} = hA(T_s - T_\infty)$, where $h$ is the convective heat transfer coefficient (W/m²K), $A$ is the surface area (m²), $T_s$ is the surface temperature, and $T_\infty$ is the bulk fluid temperature. A high $h$ means a small convective resistance.

5.1  The Extrusion Barrel

Imagine pushing cold polymer pellets into one end of a heated steel tube and pulling out a smooth, molten ribbon from the other. That is extrusion in one sentence. The screw inside the barrel conveys, melts, and pressurises the polymer. The barrel wall is where the thermal action happens: heater bands on the outside push energy in during start-up, and water-cooled jackets pull energy out during steady operation, keeping each zone at its target temperature.

The central question is: how much heat can the barrel wall transfer, and what limits it? The answer is the same one you used in Chapter 2 for a house wall in winter, but applied to steel, a polymer melt, and water — three materials in series.

Pattern Check

Before reading on — the governing equation for this chapter is $1/U = 1/h_i + L/k + 1/h_o$. Does that structure look familiar? It is identical to adding resistors in series in an electrical circuit: $R_{\text{total}} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3$. The analogy is exact — temperature difference plays the role of voltage, heat flow rate plays the role of current, and the largest resistance controls everything. You also saw this logic in chemical kinetics: when one step is much slower than the others, it sets the rate of the whole reaction. Same mathematics — three completely different physical contexts.

5.2  Barrel Zones and Thermal Goals

A single-screw extruder barrel is divided into three to five independently controlled temperature zones along its length. Each zone has its own heater band and thermocouple. Typical zone temperatures for HDPE:

Zone Location Typical Setpoint (HDPE) Purpose
Zone 1 (Feed) Near hopper 150–180 °C Begin softening without premature melt
Zone 2 (Transition) Middle 180–210 °C Complete melting
Zone 3 (Metering) Near die 200–230 °C Homogenise melt, build pressure
Die zone At die 210–240 °C Shape and surface quality

During steady-state operation, viscous dissipation (friction between the screw, melt, and barrel) generates heat. The barrel cooling jackets must remove this heat to hold the zones at setpoint. The problem becomes: how much heat can a given barrel wall cross-section transfer per unit time?

Why polymer extrusion is thermally interesting: Polymer melts have very low thermal conductivity ($k \a\approx 0.13\text{--}0.50\ \text{W/mK}$) and very high viscosity. This means the melt-side convective resistance is often the dominant bottleneck, just as a thick layer of insulation would be.

5.3  The Overall Heat Transfer Coefficient $U$

When heat passes from a polymer melt through a barrel wall into a cooling jacket, it crosses three resistances in series:

  1. Melt-side convection from the bulk melt to the inner barrel surface: resistance $1/(h_i A)$
  2. Conduction through the steel barrel wall of thickness $L$ and conductivity $k$: resistance $L/(kA)$
  3. Coolant-side convection from the outer barrel surface to the cooling water: resistance $1/(h_o A)$

By the series resistance rule from Chapter 2:

$$\frac{1}{UA} = \frac{1}{h_i A} + \frac{L}{kA} + \frac{1}{h_o A}$$

Dividing through by area $A$ gives the overall heat transfer coefficient $U$ (units W/m²K):

$$\frac{1}{U} = \frac{1}{h_i} + \frac{L}{k} + \frac{1}{h_o}$$

The heat flow across the entire barrel wall is then:

$$\dot{Q} = U A \Delta T_{\text{overall}}$$

where $\Delta T_{\text{overall}} = T_{\text{melt}} - T_{\text{coolant}}$.

Units check: Each term in $1/U$ has units of m²K/W. Check: $1/h$ has units 1/(W/m²K) = m²K/W. And $L/k$ has units m/(W/mK) = m²K/W. ✓

$U$ condenses all three resistances into a single coefficient that describes the overall ease of heat transfer. A small $U$ means the wall is thermally stiff; the melt cannot be cooled easily, regardless of how cold the water is.

Definition (always true)
$$\frac{1}{U} = \frac{1}{h_i} + \frac{L}{k} + \frac{1}{h_o}$$

Three resistances add in series; U is limited by the largest term

What governs U (dominant resistance limit)
$$U \a\approx h_i \quad \text{when } \tfrac{1}{h_i} \gg \tfrac{L}{k} + \tfrac{1}{h_o}$$

In polymer extrusion: melt side almost always dominates

Suppose $R_i = 1/h_i$ is much larger than the others. Write $R_{\text{total}} = R_i(1 + (R_w + R_o)/R_i)$. If $(R_w + R_o)/R_i \ll 1$, then $R_{\text{total}} \a\approx R_i$, so $U \a\approx 1/R_i = h_i$.

Quantitative check (Example 5.1):

$R_i = 1/200 = 0.0050$, $R_w = 0.010/10 = 0.0010$, $R_o = 1/250 = 0.0040$ (m²K/W)
$R_{\text{total}} = 0.0100$, so $R_i/R_{\text{total}} = 50\%$, $R_o/R_{\text{total}} = 40\%$.

Here melt and coolant share the burden roughly equally — neither fully dominates. But if $h_i$ drops to 50 W/m²K (viscous melt, poor mixing):

$R_i = 0.0200$, $R_{\text{total}} = 0.0250$, $R_i/R_{\text{total}} = 80\%$.

Key insight: Improving $h_o$ from 250 to 1000 W/m²K changes $R_o$ from 0.0040 to 0.0010 — but $U$ only rises from 40 to 42 W/m²K when melt side dominates. Always fix the largest resistance first.

This is the same principle as the rate-limiting step in a reaction mechanism, or the weakest link in a supply chain. The mathematics is identical.

5.4  Which Resistance Dominates?

The overall $U$ is always smaller than the smallest individual coefficient, just as the slowest link limits a supply chain. For polymer extrusion, the three terms compare very differently:

Resistance Typical value (m²K/W) Physical reason
Melt-side: $1/h_i$ 0.005–0.050 Polymer melts are viscous and have low $k$; convection is weak
Steel wall: $L/k$ 0.0002–0.002 Steel $k \a\approx 16\text{--}50$ W/mK; wall is thin; resistance is tiny
Coolant-side: $1/h_o$ 0.001–0.010 Water at moderate velocity; $h_o \a\approx 200\text{--}1000$ W/m²K

In practice, the melt-side resistance almost always dominates. Improving the barrel wall material from carbon steel to copper, for example, barely changes $U$ because the wall resistance was never the bottleneck. A better return on investment is higher screw speed (more melt mixing, higher $h_i$) or a screw design with better melt circulation.

Rate-limiting step thinking: The dominant resistance is the one worth addressing. Everything else is wasted effort. This is the same principle used in chemical kinetics (the slow step controls the rate) and in membrane separation (the layer with the lowest permeability controls flux). Identifying the limiting resistance is one of the most valuable engineering habits you will build in this course.

5.5  The Melt Energy Balance

Once you know $U$, you can compute the heat transferred per unit time. To link that to production rate, you also need the energy balance on the melt itself. If the polymer enters a barrel zone at temperature $T_1$ and exits at $T_2$:

$$\dot{Q} = \dot{m}\,c_p\,(T_2 - T_1)$$

where $\dot{m}$ is the mass flow rate (kg/s) and $c_p$ is the specific heat capacity of the polymer melt (J/kgK). This equation connects heat transfer engineering to process throughput.

Typical polymer melt properties

  • HDPE melt: $c_p \a\approx 2300\ \text{J/kgK}$, $k \a\approx 0.44\ \text{W/mK}$
  • PLA melt: $c_p \a\approx 1800\ \text{J/kgK}$, $k \a\approx 0.13\ \text{W/mK}$
  • ABS melt: $c_p \a\approx 1400\ \text{J/kgK}$, $k \a\approx 0.17\ \text{W/mK}$
  • Nylon 6 melt: $c_p \a\approx 2000\ \text{J/kgK}$, $k \a\approx 0.25\ \text{W/mK}$

The low $k$ values mean polymer melts are poor thermal conductors — heat transport within the melt depends heavily on convective mixing, not conduction.

5.6  Die and Post-Die Cooling

After the barrel, the melt enters the die where it is shaped. The die body is also heated to prevent premature solidification. Once the extrudate exits the die, it must be cooled rapidly to lock in its shape. Three post-die cooling methods are common in polymer processing:

Water bath cooling (most common for wire, pipe, and sheet): $h \a\approx 500\text{--}3000\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$. Very effective; the high $h$ of water makes this the fastest option.

Air quench (film extrusion, blow moulding): $h \a\approx 20\text{--}80\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$. Gentler, gives more control over crystallinity. The low $h$ means longer cooling times.

Chill roll contact (cast film, sheet): $h_{\text{contact}} \a\approx 200\text{--}1000\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$. The good metal-to-metal contact provides effective cooling with precise surface finish control.

In each case, the same $U$ framework applies, with the appropriate $h$ values for the contact medium. The polymer thermal conductivity appears as the internal conduction resistance when the extrudate is thick.

Why cooling rate matters for polymer structure: Slow cooling of semi-crystalline polymers (HDPE, nylon) allows more crystallinity to develop, giving higher stiffness and density. Rapid quenching produces less crystallinity, giving more transparency and toughness. Controlling $U$ controls microstructure.

5.8  Worked Examples

Example 5.1 — Overall $U$ for an HDPE Extruder Barrel

An HDPE extruder barrel has a steel wall 10 mm thick ($k = 10\ \text{W/mK}$). The melt-side convective coefficient is $h_i = 200\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$ and the cooling-water side is $h_o = 250\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$. Compute $U$.

Solution:

$$\frac{1}{U} = \frac{1}{h_i} + \frac{L}{k} + \frac{1}{h_o} = \frac{1}{200} + \frac{0.010}{10} + \frac{1}{250}$$ $$= 0.0050 + 0.0010 + 0.0040 = 0.0100\ \text{m}^2\text{K/W}$$ $$U = 100\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$$

Note that the wall resistance (0.0010) is only 10% of the total. Even replacing the steel with copper ($k = 400\ \text{W/mK}$) would change the wall term from 0.0010 to 0.000025 — negligible impact on $U$.

Example 5.2 — Throughput from an Energy Balance

A cooling zone (barrel area $A = 0.5\ \text{m}^2$) operates with $U = 100\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$, melt temperature 180 °C, and coolant temperature 80 °C. The melt (HDPE, $c_p = 2000\ \text{J/kgK}$) drops 25 °C as it passes through this zone. What is the mass throughput $\dot{m}$?

Solution:

Heat removed by the barrel wall: $$\dot{Q} = UA\Delta T = 100 \times 0.5 \times (180 - 80) = 5000\ \text{W}$$

Energy balance on the melt: $$\dot{Q} = \dot{m}\,c_p\,\Delta T_{\text{melt}} \Rightarrow \dot{m} = \frac{5000}{2000 \times 25} = 0.10\ \text{kg/s}$$

The extruder delivers $0.10\ \text{kg/s} = 360\ \text{kg/h}$ of HDPE through this zone.

C5
Hard

Derive the dominant-resistance approximation algebraically and apply it.

(a) Starting from $1/U = R_i + R_w + R_o$ (where $R_i = 1/h_i$, etc.), factor out $R_i$ to write $U = h_i / (1 + (R_w + R_o)/R_i)$. Define the ratio $ \varepsilon = (R_w + R_o)/R_i$ and show that when $ \varepsilon \ll 1$, $U \approx h_i\,(1 - \varepsilon)$. What does this tell you about the marginal benefit of reducing a small resistance?

(b) For the system: $h_i = 100\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$, $L/k = 0.001\ \text{m}^2\text{K/W}$, $h_o = 400\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$. Calculate $U$, then compute $ \varepsilon$. Is the dominant-resistance approximation valid here?

(c) An engineer doubles $h_o$ from 400 to 800 W/m²K (50% more pump energy). By what absolute and relative amount does $U$ increase? Then doubles $h_i$ from 100 to 200 W/m²K (new screw design, same pump energy). Compare the two improvements. Which investment was more effective, and why?

(d) At what value of $h_o$ (keeping $h_i = 100$ and $L/k = 0.001$) would the coolant resistance become negligible (say, contributing less than 5% of total resistance)? Show your calculation.

(a) Dominant-resistance algebra: $U = 1/(R_i + R_w + R_o) = (1/R_i) \times 1/(1 + (R_w+R_o)/R_i) = h_i/(1+ \varepsilon)$. For $ \varepsilon \ll 1$: $U \approx h_i(1- \varepsilon)$. Each unit reduction in a small resistance improves $U$ by only $h_i^2 \times \delta R$ — a second-order effect. Reducing a resistance that is already small barely moves $U$.

(b) Numerical check: $R_i=0.0100$, $R_w=0.0010$, $R_o=0.0025$, $R_{\text{tot}}=0.0135$, $U = 74.1\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$. $ \varepsilon = (0.0010+0.0025)/0.0100 = 0.35$. Not small — the approximation gives $U \approx 100 \times (1-0.35) = 65$ vs exact 74: rough but directionally correct.

(c) Two improvements: Double $h_o$: $R_o = 0.00125$, $U = 81.6$ (+10%). Double $h_i$: $R_i = 0.005$, $U = 117.6$ (+59%). Improving the dominant resistance is ~6× more effective per unit of effort.

(d) Threshold $h_o$: Need $R_o < 0.05\,(R_i + R_w + R_o)$. Aapproximating $R_{\text{tot}} \approx 0.011$: $R_o < 0.05 \times 0.011 = 5.5\times10^{-4}$, so $h_o > 1/5.5\times10^{-4} \approx 1820\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$. This is in the range of fast-flowing water or dilute glycol — achievable, but only worthwhile once the melt side has been addressed first.

Bridge → Lab

In your unit operations lab, a single-screw extruder running HDPE at 200 kg/h is supposed to maintain Zone 3 at 220 °C. The temperature controller shows the zone is consistently running 15 °C above setpoint even with the cooling jackets fully open.

(a) List the three resistances in the cooling circuit and, without calculating, identify which one you suspect is most likely limiting the cooling. Justify your reasoning from the physical properties of HDPE melt vs. water.

(b) The cooling water inlet is 20 °C and the outlet temperature is measured at 45 °C. The water flow rate is 8 L/min ($\dot{m} = 0.133\ \text{kg/s}$, $c_p = 4200\ \text{J/kgK}$). Compute the actual heat removal rate $\dot{Q}_{\text{water}}$.

(c) Estimate the expected $\dot{Q}$ from viscous dissipation: HDPE melt specific heat $c_p = 2300\ \text{J/kgK}$, throughput $\dot{m} = 0.056\ \text{kg/s}$ (200 kg/h), melt temperature excess $\Delta T_{\text{melt}} = 15\,°\text{C}$. Compare with your answer to (b) and diagnose the problem.

(d) Propose two specific, actionable changes to fix the overheating. For each, state which resistance in the $1/U$ equation you are targeting.

(a) Limiting resistance: The melt-side resistance ($1/h_i$). HDPE melt has $k \approx 0.44\ \text{W/mK}$ and $\mu \sim 1000\ \text{Pa}\cdot\text{s}$ at 220 °C. These give very poor internal convection ($h_i \sim 50\text{--}200\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$) compared to water ($h_o \sim 500\text{--}2000\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$). The melt side almost certainly limits.

(b) Actual cooling by water: $$\dot{Q}_{\text{water}} = \dot{m}\,c_p\,\Delta T = 0.133 \times 4200 \times 25 = 13{,}965\ \text{W} \approx 14\ \text{kW}$$ (c) Heat that needs removing: $$\dot{Q}_{\text{needed}} = \dot{m}_{\text{melt}}\,c_p\,\Delta T_{\text{melt}} = 0.056 \times 2300 \times 15 = 1932\ \text{W} \approx 1.9\ \text{kW}$$ The cooling water is already removing 14 kW, but the melt only needs 1.9 kW removed. The water capacity is not the problem. The melt temperature excess means the heat is being generated (by viscous dissipation) faster than it can cross the melt-side boundary layer to reach the wall.

(d) Two fixes:
• Reduce screw speed (targets viscous dissipation source): Less shear = less heat generated. If the throughput allows it, dropping screw speed by 20% reduces viscous heating roughly proportionally.
• Install a mixing screw section (targets $h_i$, the melt-side resistance): Better melt mixing increases $h_i$ from ~100 to ~300 W/m²K, tripling the melt-side conductance and improving $U$ significantly.

5.11  Chapter Summary

Key Equations

Quantity Formula Notes
Overall heat transfer coefficient $1/U = 1/h_i + L/k + 1/h_o$ Flat geometry; add more terms for more layers
Heat flow $\dot{Q} = UA\Delta T_{\text{overall}}$ $\Delta T$ from melt to coolant bulk
Melt energy balance $\dot{Q} = \dot{m}\,c_p\,\Delta T_{\text{melt}}$ Links heat transfer to throughput
Dominant resistance $R_{\text{dominant}} = \max(1/h_i,\ L/k,\ 1/h_o)$ Controls $U$; target this one first

Key Ideas

  • The overall heat transfer coefficient $U$ combines all resistances in series: melt convection, wall conduction, and coolant convection.
  • In polymer extrusion, the melt-side resistance always dominates because polymer melts have low $k$ and high viscosity.
  • Improving the wall material (carbon steel → copper) barely changes $U$ when the melt side dominates. Target the largest resistance.
  • The melt energy balance $\dot{Q} = \dot{m}\,c_p\,\Delta T$ connects heat transfer engineering to production throughput.
  • Post-die cooling method determines cooling rate: water bath $\gg$ chill roll $\gg$ air quench; this controls polymer crystallinity and final properties.
💡 Memory anchor: “$1/U = 1/h_i + L/k + 1/h_o$. The largest term wins. For polymer extrusion, it is almost always the melt side. Knowing that tells you where to look when cooling is too slow.”
