Chapter 6

Heat Transfer in Injection Molding

Injection molding is one of the highest-volume polymer manufacturing processes in the world. Of the total cycle time, roughly 70–80% is spent cooling the part to a temperature where it can be ejected without warping. Heat transfer analysis is therefore the single most important lever for improving throughput. This chapter develops the tools to quantify it.

✍
Cold Recall — Before You Read

Close your notes. Try each question from memory, then reveal the answer. The ones you cannot recall are exactly what this chapter reinforces.

RC-1. Write the expression for $T_{\max}$ inside a slab of thickness $2L$ with uniform internal heat generation $\dot{q}$ (W/m³), cooled symmetrically on both faces to temperature $T_w$. What does this expression tell you about which material properties matter most?

$$T_{\max} = T_w + \frac{\dot{q}\,L^2}{2k}$$ The maximum temperature excess above the wall depends on $\dot{q}$ (heat source strength), $L^2$ (geometry), and $k$ (thermal conductivity). A low-$k$ material like a polymer traps heat more effectively than steel. Note: in injection molding there is no internal heat generation — the “source” is the latent heat and sensible heat already stored in the melt.

RC-2. In Chapter 5 you built the formula $1/U = 1/h_i + L/k + 1/h_o$. Identify what each of the three terms represents physically. What determines which term dominates?

$1/h_i$ = convective resistance on the hot (melt or part) side. $L/k$ = conductive resistance through the wall or part itself. $1/h_o$ = convective resistance on the cold (coolant) side. The largest term dominates: it is the rate-limiting step for heat removal. In polymer processing, the polymer's low $k$ often makes the part itself the limiting resistance once solidification begins.

RC-3. State Newton's law of cooling. What are the units of the convective coefficient $h$? Name one practical way to increase $h$ in a mold cooling channel.

$$\dot{Q} = hA(T_s - T_{\infty})$$ $h$ has units W/m²K. To increase $h$ in a mold cooling channel: increase the coolant velocity (higher Re, thinner boundary layer) or use a coolant with higher $k$ or Prandtl number. Turbulent flow in the channel (Re > 4000) gives a far higher $h$ than laminar flow.

6.1  The Cycle Time Problem

A nylon bracket injection mold runs at 4 shots per minute in your factory. Your competitor runs the same part at 6 shots per minute. Their cost per part is 33% lower, and the difference is almost entirely in cycle time — specifically, in how fast they cool the mold.

Injection molding cycle time has four phases: mold close and injection (∼5% of cycle), packing and hold (∼15%), cooling (∼70%), and mold open/part eject (∼10%). If you want to speed up the process, cooling is the only phase large enough to make a significant difference. That is a heat transfer problem.

This chapter develops the tools to answer: how much heat must be removed from the mold per shot, how fast can the mold wall remove it, and which material properties determine whether the cycle time is 5 seconds or 30 seconds.

Pattern Check

Before reading on — the key result of this chapter is $t_{\text{cool}} \propto s^2/\alpha$. Does that ratio look familiar? From Chapter 8 you know the Fourier number $Fo = \alpha\,t/s^2$: it is the dimensionless time for transient conduction in a slab. This chapter just inverts it: to find how long cooling takes, solve $Fo \sim 1$ for $t$, giving $t \sim s^2/\alpha$. The $s^2$ is the universal signature of diffusion — it appears in heat conduction, mass diffusion, and even random walks. Halve the wall thickness, quarter the cooling time. That scaling is worth memorising cold.

6.2  The Injection Molding Cycle

Molten polymer enters the cold mold cavity under high pressure (typically 50–200 MPa). The polymer begins to solidify immediately on contact with the cold mold wall. A frozen skin forms and thickens inward as the hot core continues to transfer heat outward through the solidified layer and into the mold steel.

The part can be ejected only when the entire cross-section has cooled below the ejection temperature $T_e$ — the temperature at which the polymer is stiff enough not to distort under ejection force. Typical values:

Polymer Melt injection temp (°C) Ejection temp (°C) $\Delta T$ removed (°C)
Nylon 6/628080200
HDPE22060160
ABS24075165
PLA20060140
Polycarbonate300120180

Notice that a large $\Delta T$ must be removed each shot. For a 200-g nylon part with $c_p = 2000\ \text{J/kgK}$: the sensible heat alone is $Q = 0.2 \times 2000 \times 200 = 80\ \text{kJ}$ per shot. For semi-crystalline polymers (HDPE, nylon), the latent heat of crystallisation adds another 10–30% on top.

6.3  Heat Content of the Shot

The total heat to be removed from one part (one shot) is:

$$Q_{\text{shot}} = m\,c_p\,(T_{\text{melt}} - T_e)$$

For semi-crystalline polymers, add the latent heat of crystallisation $H_c$ (J/kg):

$$Q_{\text{shot}} = m\bigl[c_p(T_{\text{melt}} - T_e) + H_c\bigr]$$

Typical $H_c$ values: HDPE 180 kJ/kg; nylon 6/6 80 kJ/kg; PLA 40 kJ/kg; ABS 0 (amorphous, no latent heat).

This heat must all pass through the mold wall and into the cooling channels before the part can be ejected. $Q_{\text{shot}}$ sets the minimum work the cooling system must do per cycle.

Where does the heat come from? All of it was put in upstream: the extruder barrel heaters melted the pellets, stored sensible and latent heat in the melt, and injected that thermal energy into the mold cavity. The mold cooling system's job is to extract that stored energy fast enough to meet the cycle time target.

6.4  Mold Cooling: The Overall Heat Transfer Coefficient

Heat flows from the solidifying part through the mold steel and into the cooling channel coolant. Using the series resistance model from Chapter 5:

$$\frac{1}{U} = \frac{L_{\text{mold}}}{k_{\text{mold}}} + \frac{1}{h_{\text{coolant}}}$$

Here the contact resistance between the solidified part and the mold wall is often small and is neglected in a first analysis. $L_{\text{mold}}$ is the distance from the cavity surface to the cooling channel, and $k_{\text{mold}}$ is the mold material thermal conductivity.

Mold material $k$ (W/mK) Relative cooling speed
Tool steel (P20)30–37Standard
Stainless steel 31616Slower (−40%)
Aluminium 7075130–150Fast (×4)
Beryllium copper100–130Fast (×3)
Copper alloy (MoldMAX)200Fastest (×5)

In injection molding, the mold wall conductive resistance is more significant than in the extruder barrel because the distance to the cooling channel is typically longer (10–30 mm) and the contact area matters enormously. Channel placement is a critical design parameter.

Coolant-side $h$ for injection mold channels: Water flowing in a 10 mm diameter channel at 2 m/s is turbulent ($Re \approx 20{,}000$) and gives $h \approx 3000\text{--}8000\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$. If flow is too slow (laminar), $h$ drops by 10–50$\times$. Always verify turbulent flow in mold channels.

6.5  Estimating Cooling Time

A simple but useful estimate of minimum cooling time comes from dividing the total heat stored in the part by the maximum rate at which the mold can extract it:

$$t_{\text{cool}} \approx \frac{Q_{\text{shot}}}{\dot{Q}_{\text{max}}} = \frac{m\,c_p\,(T_{\text{melt}} - T_e)}{U\,A\,(T_{\text{melt}} - T_{\text{coolant}})}$$

This is an optimistic lower bound because it assumes the full $\Delta T$ is available throughout cooling. In practice, as the part cools, $\Delta T$ decreases and the heat extraction rate falls. The true cooling time is 1.5–2$\times$ this estimate for most parts.

A more rigorous approach uses the transient conduction solution (Chapter 8) for a slab of half-thickness $s$ cooled on both faces:

$$t_{\text{cool}} = \frac{s^2}{\pi^2\,\alpha}\ln\!\left(\frac{4}{\pi} \cdot\frac{T_{\text{melt}} - T_{\text{mold}}}{T_e - T_{\text{mold}}}\right)$$

where $\alpha = k/(\rho c_p)$ is the thermal diffusivity of the polymer and $T_{\text{mold}}$ is the mold wall temperature. This formula (Ballman–Shusman approximation) shows that cooling time scales as $s^2 / \alpha$. Halving the wall thickness cuts cooling time by 4$\times$ — wall thickness is by far the strongest lever available to the part designer.

Design rule of thumb: In injection molding, cooling time scales as (wall thickness)². A 3 mm wall takes 4$\times$ as long to cool as a 1.5 mm wall. This is why thin-wall injection molding is such a powerful cycle-time reduction strategy.
Exact formula (Ballman–Shusman)
$$t_c = \frac{s^2}{\pi^2\,\alpha}\ln\!\left(\frac{4}{\pi}\cdot\frac{T_m - T_w}{T_e - T_w}\right)$$

Log factor accounts for temperature ratio; typically 1.5–3 for real processes

Design scaling rule
$$t_c \propto \frac{s^2}{\alpha} \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad Fo = \frac{\alpha\,t_c}{s^2} \approx \text{const}$$

$s^2$ is the diffusion signature — halve $s$, quarter $t$

Start from the 1-D heat diffusion equation inside the polymer slab:

$$\frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \alpha\,\frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial x^2}$$

Step 1 — Identify the scales: Temperature varies by $\Delta T = T_m - T_w$ over a spatial scale $s$ (half-thickness). So $\partial^2 T/\partial x^2 \sim \Delta T/s^2$, and the equation reads $\Delta T/t \sim \alpha\,\Delta T/s^2$.

Step 2 — Solve for the time scale: $$t \sim \frac{s^2}{\alpha}$$ This is the only combination of $s$ and $\alpha$ that has units of time. No other grouping is possible on dimensional grounds.

Step 3 — Non-dimensionalise (connect to Chapter 8): Define $\theta = (T-T_w)/(T_m-T_w)$ and $Fo = \alpha t/s^2$. The equation becomes $\partial\theta/\partial Fo = \partial^2\theta/\partial\xi^2$ (where $\xi = x/s$). There is no dimensional parameter left — so all slabs at the same $Fo$ are at identical stages of cooling, regardless of material or thickness. Ejection requires $\theta_{\text{centre}} \le \theta_e$, which means a fixed $Fo^*$, giving $t_c = Fo^* \cdot s^2/\alpha$. ✓

The log factor in Ballman–Shusman is $\pi^2 Fo^* = \ln(4/\pi \cdot \Delta T_{\text{ratio}})$ — it is just the series solution for the centre temperature evaluated at the ejection condition.

6.6  Thermal Diffusivity and Why It Controls Cycle Time

The cooling time formula contains $\alpha = k / (\rho c_p)$, the thermal diffusivity (m²/s). Higher $\alpha$ means faster heat penetration through the part, hence faster cooling. Thermal diffusivity combines all three relevant material properties:

$$\alpha = \frac{k}{\rho\,c_p}$$

$k$ (W/mK) is the ability to conduct heat; $\rho c_p$ (J/m³K) is the heat stored per unit volume per degree. High $\alpha$ means the material conducts heat quickly relative to how much it stores — it is a “fast” thermal material.

Material $k$ (W/mK) $\rho c_p$ (MJ/m³K) $\alpha$ (m²/s) Relative to HDPE
Copper4003.41.2×10−4500×
Tool steel353.51.0×10−540×
HDPE0.441.92.3×10−71×
Nylon 60.252.01.3×10−70.56×
PLA0.132.35.7×10−80.25×

This table explains why PLA parts take roughly 4$\times$ longer to cool than HDPE parts of the same thickness: PLA has both lower $k$ and higher $\rho c_p$. A process engineer moving from HDPE to PLA must adjust cycle times accordingly or reduce wall thickness to compensate.

6.7  Cooling System Design Principles

Four variables control how quickly the mold removes heat from a part. In order of their typical engineering leverage:

1. Wall thickness (part design, scales as $s^2$): the strongest lever. Reducing wall thickness from 3 mm to 2 mm cuts cooling time by more than half. Requires close collaboration between part designer and tool designer.

2. Cooling channel proximity (mold design, reduces $L_{\text{mold}}$): channels drilled closer to the cavity surface reduce the mold wall conductive resistance. Conformal cooling channels (produced by metal additive manufacturing) can follow the cavity geometry and deliver dramatic improvements.

3. Coolant flow rate (process parameter, raises $h_{\text{coolant}}$): ensure turbulent flow in all channels (Re > 4000). Turbulent flow gives $h \approx 3000$–8000 W/m²K vs. laminar $h \approx 300$–800 W/m²K.

4. Mold material (tool design, changes $k_{\text{mold}}$): aluminium or beryllium-copper inserts at high-heat areas can reduce local cycle time. However, the mold wall resistance is often not the dominant one, so this has diminishing returns once channels are close.

Conformal cooling — mold channels manufactured by metal 3D printing that precisely follow the cavity contour — represents the largest recent advance in injection mold cooling. Cycle time reductions of 30–50% are achievable for complex geometries where conventional drilled channels cannot reach.

6.9  Worked Examples

Example 6.1 — Heat Content of a Nylon Shot

A nylon 6/6 part has mass 0.20 kg. Injection temperature: 230 °C. Ejection temperature: 80 °C. Specific heat of melt: $c_p = 2000\ \text{J/kgK}$. Latent heat of crystallisation: $H_c = 60\ \text{kJ/kg}$. Find total heat to remove per shot.

Solution:

Sensible heat: $Q_s = 0.20 \times 2000 \times (230 - 80) = 0.20 \times 2000 \times 150 = 60{,}000\ \text{J}$

Latent heat: $Q_L = 0.20 \times 60{,}000 = 12{,}000\ \text{J}$

$$Q_{\text{shot}} = 60{,}000 + 12{,}000 = 72{,}000\ \text{J} = 72\ \text{kJ}$$

At 4 shots/min, the cooling system must handle $72{,}000 \times 4 / 60 = 4800\ \text{W} = 4.8\ \text{kW}$ continuously.

Example 6.2 — Mold Wall $U$ and Heat Removal Rate

A tool-steel mold has a cavity-to-channel distance $L = 20\ \text{mm}$, $k = 40\ \text{W/mK}$. Cooling water gives $h_o = 500\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$. The cavity face area is $A = 0.10\ \text{m}^2$. Melt at 230 °C, coolant at 30 °C. Find $U$ and the heat removal rate.

Solution:

$$\frac{1}{U} = \frac{L}{k} + \frac{1}{h_o} = \frac{0.020}{40} + \frac{1}{500} = 0.0005 + 0.0020 = 0.0025\ \text{m}^2\text{K/W}$$ $$U = 400\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$$

$$\dot{Q} = UA\Delta T = 400 \times 0.10 \times (230 - 30) = 8000\ \text{W} = 8\ \text{kW}$$

C5
Hard

From the diffusion equation to the Ballman–Shusman formula. This problem builds the full chain: governing PDE → dimensionless group → closed-form cooling time.

(a) Write the 1-D transient heat conduction equation $\partial T/\partial t = \alpha\,\partial^2 T/\partial x^2$ for a slab of half-thickness $s$. Non-dimensionalise using $\theta = (T-T_w)/(T_m-T_w)$, $\xi = x/s$, and $\tau = \alpha\,t/s^2$. What dimensionless group naturally emerges as the dimensionless time, and what is its physical meaning?

(b) The first-term series solution at the centre of the slab ($\xi=0$) gives $$\theta_c \approx \frac{4}{\pi}\exp(-\pi^2\,Fo), \quad Fo = \frac{\alpha\,t}{s^2}.$$ Define the ejection criterion $\theta_e = (T_e - T_w)/(T_m - T_w)$. Solve for $Fo^*$ at which $\theta_c = \theta_e$, then convert back to real time $t_c = Fo^* \cdot s^2/\alpha$ to derive the Ballman–Shusman formula.

(c) For HDPE: $T_m = 230\,°\text{C}$, $T_w = 25\,°\text{C}$, $T_e = 80\,°\text{C}$, $s = 2\ \text{mm}$, $\alpha = 2.3\times10^{-7}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}$. Calculate $\theta_e$, then $Fo^*$, then $t_c$ numerically.

(d) A quick engineering rule sets $Fo \sim 1$ to estimate $t \sim s^2/\alpha$. Using your value of $Fo^*$ from (c), by what factor does the exact result differ from this rough estimate? Why is the rough estimate still valuable despite the factor?

(a) Non-dimensionalisation. Substituting $\theta$, $\xi$, $\tau$ into the PDE: $$\frac{\partial\theta}{\partial\tau} = \frac{\partial^2\theta}{\partial\xi^2}.$$ The only dimensionless group is the Fourier number $Fo = \tau = \alpha\,t/s^2$, which is the ratio of heat diffused so far ($\alpha\,t$) to the distance the heat must travel ($s^2$). When $Fo \sim 1$ the temperature profile has penetrated the full thickness.

(b) Deriving the Ballman–Shusman formula. Set $\theta_c = \theta_e$: $$\theta_e = \frac{4}{\pi}\exp(-\pi^2\,Fo^*) \implies Fo^* = \frac{1}{\pi^2}\ln\!\left(\frac{4/\pi}{\theta_e}\right) = \frac{1}{\pi^2}\ln\!\left(\frac{4}{\pi}\cdot\frac{T_m-T_w}{T_e-T_w}\right).$$ Converting back: $t_c = Fo^* \cdot s^2/\alpha$, which gives $$\boxed{t_c = \frac{s^2}{\pi^2\alpha}\ln\!\left(\frac{4}{\pi}\cdot\frac{T_m-T_w}{T_e-T_w}\right).}$$ This is the Ballman–Shusman formula — it emerges directly from non-dimensionalising the diffusion equation and inverting the leading-term Fourier series.

(c) Numerical check for HDPE. $$\theta_e = \frac{80-25}{230-25} = \frac{55}{205} \approx 0.268.$$ $$Fo^* = \frac{1}{\pi^2}\ln\!\left(\frac{4}{\pi\times0.268}\right) = \frac{1}{9.870}\ln(4.775) \approx \frac{1.563}{9.870} \approx 0.158.$$ $$t_c = 0.158 \times \frac{(0.002)^2}{2.3\times10^{-7}} = 0.158 \times 17.39 \approx \mathbf{2.75\ \text{s}}.$$ (The interactive widget gives ≈ 2.8 s — excellent agreement.)

(d) Rough estimate vs exact. The crude rule $Fo \sim 1 \Rightarrow t \sim s^2/\alpha = 17.4\ \text{s}$ overestimates by $1/Fo^* \approx 6\times$. The exact prefactor is $Fo^* \approx 0.16$, not 1. Despite the factor-of-6 error, the rough rule is useful because: (i) it gives the correct scaling — $t \propto s^2/\alpha$ is exact; (ii) a factor of 6 is irrelevant when comparing materials or wall thicknesses (ratios cancel); and (iii) it requires no temperatures, making it a fast first-pass screen before detailed optimisation.

B1
Bridge → Lab

In your unit operations injection molding lab, a polycarbonate housing (wall thickness 3.5 mm) is warping on ejection. The mold temperature is set to 90 °C (required for PC to fill properly), and the mold uses water at 80 °C. The melt temperature is 310 °C and the target ejection temperature is 130 °C. Current cycle time is 45 seconds. The lab supervisor asks you to determine if the warping is a thermal problem or a packing/flow problem.

(a) Estimate the minimum cooling time for this part using $\alpha_{\text{PC}} = 1.2\times10^{-7}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}$, $s = 1.75\ \text{mm}$ (half-thickness). Use the Ballman–Shusman formula with $T_{\text{mold}} = 90\,°\text{C}$.

(b) Compare your estimate to the 45-second cycle. Is the cooling time consistent with adequate cooling, or is the part being ejected too early?

(c) The operator suggests reducing the mold temperature to 40 °C to speed cooling. What are the risks of this approach for a polycarbonate part? Suggest a safer alternative that addresses the root cause.

(a) Ballman–Shusman cooling time:
$s = 0.00175\ \text{m}$, $\alpha = 1.2\times10^{-7}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}$
$s^2/\alpha = (0.00175)^2 / (1.2\times10^{-7}) = 3.0625\times10^{-6}/1.2\times10^{-7} = 25.5\ \text{s}$
$\Delta T_{\text{ratio}} = (310 - 90)/(130 - 90) = 220/40 = 5.5$
$\ln(4/\pi \times 5.5) = \ln(7.00) = 1.946$
$t_c = (25.5/\pi^2) \times 1.946 = (25.5/9.87) \times 1.946 = 2.583 \times 1.946 \approx 5.0\ \text{s}$

Wait — this seems short. Let me recalculate: $s^2/(\pi^2\alpha) = 25.5/9.87 = 2.58\ \text{s}$; then $\times \ln(4/\pi \times 5.5) = \times 1.946$, giving $t_c \approx 5.0\ \text{s}$.

(b) Comparison: The minimum cooling time estimate is about 5 seconds. The cycle is running at 45 seconds — far longer than the thermal minimum. The cooling time itself is not likely the cause of warping. The part has plenty of time to cool thermally at 45 seconds.

(c) Root cause analysis: With 45 seconds and 5-second thermal minimum, warping is likely caused by one of:
  • Residual stress from packing: over-packing or under-packing creates a frozen-in stress field that relaxes on ejection.
  • Non-uniform cooling: one side of the part cooling faster than the other creates a temperature gradient that drives bending on ejection.
  • Ejection before $T_g$ fully reached: PC $T_g \approx 147\,°\text{C}$; if any interior region is above 130 °C, it can relax under ejection force.
Lowering mold temperature from 90 to 40 °C risks poor surface finish and short shots in PC (it needs a warm mold to fill long thin sections), and may increase residual stress by increasing the $\Delta T$ through the part cross-section.

Safer alternative: Check that both mold halves (core and cavity) are at the same temperature. Temperature imbalance between the two halves is the most common cause of warping in thick PC parts.

Key Ideas

  • Cooling occupies 70–80% of the injection molding cycle; it is the primary lever for cycle time improvement.
  • Cooling time scales as $s^2/\alpha$: wall thickness squared is the strongest design variable.
  • Thermal diffusivity $\alpha = k/(\rho c_p)$ determines how fast heat conducts out of the polymer. PLA is about 4$\times$ slower than HDPE.
  • The mold wall overall U combines conductive resistance (mold steel) and convective resistance (coolant channel). Turbulent coolant flow is critical.
  • Semi-crystalline polymers release latent heat of crystallisation during cooling, adding significantly to the heat per shot.
  • Conformal cooling channels reduce $L_{\text{mold}}$, cutting the conductive resistance and enabling 30–50% cycle time reductions.
