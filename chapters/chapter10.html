Chapter 10

Natural Convection: Buoyancy, Rayleigh Numbers, and Churchill–Chu Correlations

In forced convection a fan or pump drives the flow. But what happens when there is no external driver at all? Hot surfaces still cool, and cold surfaces still warm, even in perfectly still air. The engine is buoyancy: warm fluid is less dense, rises, pulls cool fluid in from below, and a self-sustaining circulation forms with no motor required. This chapter quantifies that process.

✍
Cold Recall — Before You Read

Close your notes. Try each question from memory, then reveal the answer. The ones you cannot recall are exactly what this chapter reinforces.

RC-1. In forced convection over a flat plate at $Re = 10^5$ and $Pr = 0.71$, you computed $h \approx 9.7\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$. What two things would you change to get a higher $h$?

Increase flow velocity (raises Re, thins the boundary layer) or switch to a fluid with higher $k_f$ or Pr (e.g., water instead of air). Both thin or alter the effective boundary layer thickness that controls heat flow.

RC-2. What is the key difference between forced convection and natural (free) convection?

In forced convection, an external agent (fan, pump, moving belt) drives the fluid flow. In natural convection, fluid motion arises entirely from buoyancy: density differences caused by temperature gradients, with gravity acting on those differences. No external mover is needed.

RC-3. Why does a hot surface in still air produce an upward current? What two material properties determine how strong that current is?

The hot surface warms adjacent air, reducing its density. Gravity pulls denser cool air down, which displaces the lighter warm air upward. The strength of the current depends on (1) the thermal expansion coefficient $\beta$ (how much density changes per degree) and (2) the kinematic viscosity $\nu$ (which resists the flow). Large $\beta$, small $\nu$ → strong current.

10.1  The Cooling Oven Door

You open a polymer-processing oven and leave the door ajar. Within minutes the metal surface that was at 200 °C has cooled enough to touch. No fan runs. The room air is still. Yet heat leaves the surface at a steady rate. Where does the energy go?

Touch the side of any hot oven: you feel a column of warm air rising. That column is natural (free) convection. The hot surface warms a thin layer of air, making it less dense. Buoyancy lifts it; cooler room air slides in to replace it. A circulation loop forms with no pump, consuming only the thermal energy stored in the surface.

For most polymer processes — quiescent melt pools, oven interiors, jacketed reactors left idle, cooling flanges in still air — natural convection is the dominant heat-transfer mode on the gas side. The heat-transfer coefficients it produces are modest (typically $h \approx 2\text{--}25\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$ in air), but understanding them is essential for predicting cooling rates, set-point temperatures, and energy losses.

Pattern Check

Before reading on — you already know that $Re$ is the single number that governs whether forced convection flow is laminar or turbulent. This chapter introduces $Ra_L = Gr_L \cdot Pr$, which plays exactly the same role in natural convection. The key difference: in forced convection you set the velocity externally; in natural convection buoyancy sets it for you. Same mathematics, completely different physics — and that is precisely what makes Ra so elegant.

10.2  Buoyancy: The Driving Force

Consider a vertical heated plate at surface temperature $T_s$ in quiescent fluid at $T_\infty < T_s$. The fluid directly adjacent to the plate is heated, its density drops, and buoyancy generates an upward body force per unit volume:

$$f_b = \rho g \beta (T - T_\infty)$$

Here $\beta$ is the volumetric thermal expansion coefficient (units K$^{-1}$) and $g = 9.81\ \text{m/s}^2$. This buoyancy force drives the upward flow, while viscosity $\mu$ resists it. The competition between buoyancy and viscosity determines whether the resulting flow is laminar or turbulent.

Unlike forced convection, the flow velocity in natural convection is not an independent variable — it is determined by the buoyancy force itself. This makes natural convection inherently coupled: the temperature field drives the flow, and the flow transports heat.

Key insight: Natural convection $h$ values are typically 5–20$\times$ smaller than forced convection $h$ values for the same geometry. This is why adding a fan to a polymer cooling system can dramatically shorten cycle times.

10.3  The Grashof Number

The Grashof number $Gr_L$ plays the role in natural convection that $Re$ plays in forced convection. It is the ratio of buoyancy forces to viscous forces:

$$Gr_L = \frac{g\,\beta\,\Delta T\,L^3}{\nu^2}$$

where $L$ is the characteristic length (plate height for a vertical surface), $\Delta T = T_s - T_\infty$, and $\nu$ is kinematic viscosity. Notice the $L^3$ dependence: a surface twice as tall produces $2^3 = 8\times$ stronger buoyancy-driven flow.

The laminar–turbulent transition for a vertical plate occurs near $Gr_L \sim 10^9$.

All fluid properties are evaluated at the film temperature:

$$T_{\text{film}} = \frac{T_s + T_\infty}{2}$$

10.4  The Rayleigh Number

In practice, the Grashof number almost always appears multiplied by the Prandtl number. This product is the Rayleigh number:

$$Ra_L = Gr_L \cdot Pr = \frac{g\,\beta\,\Delta T\,L^3}{\nu\,\alpha}$$

where $\alpha = k/(\rho c_p)$ is the thermal diffusivity. Physically, $Ra_L$ captures how much buoyancy-driven inertia exists relative to the product of viscous and thermal diffusion that both damp the flow. All empirical correlations for natural convection are expressed in terms of $Ra_L$.

Physical interpretation of Ra

  • $Ra \lesssim 10^4$: Conduction dominates; buoyancy too weak to generate significant flow.
  • $10^4 \lesssim Ra \lesssim 10^9$: Laminar natural convection; most practical engineering cases.
  • $Ra \gtrsim 10^9$: Turbulent natural convection; tall surfaces, large $\Delta T$.
Definition (always true)
$$Ra_L = \frac{g\,\beta\,\Delta T\,L^3}{\nu\,\alpha}$$

Buoyancy driving force ÷ (viscous × thermal damping)

What governs Nu (from scaling)
$$\overline{Nu}_L \sim Ra_L^{1/4} \quad \text{(laminar)}$$

Boundary layer thickens as $L^{3/4}$; local $h$ decreases up the plate

We want to find what combination of parameters governs the heat-transfer coefficient when there is no externally set velocity. Start from the buoyancy–momentum balance in the boundary layer of thickness $\delta$ on a vertical plate of height $L$:

Step 1 — Velocity scale from buoyancy vs. viscosity: Buoyancy force per unit volume: $f_b \sim \rho g\beta\Delta T$. Viscous resistance: $f_v \sim \mu\,u/\delta^2$. Balancing these: $$u \sim \frac{g\beta\Delta T\,\delta^2}{\nu}$$

Step 2 — Thermal boundary-layer thickness: Balance convective heat transport $(\sim u\,\Delta T / L)$ against conductive transport $(\sim \alpha\,\Delta T/\delta_T^2)$ in the energy equation: $$\delta_T \sim L\,Ra_L^{-1/4}, \quad \text{where} \quad Ra_L = \frac{g\,\beta\,\Delta T\,L^3}{\nu\,\alpha}$$

Step 3 — Nu follows directly: $h \sim k_f/\delta_T \sim k_f\,Ra_L^{1/4}/L$, so $$\overline{Nu}_L = \frac{hL}{k_f} \sim Ra_L^{1/4} \quad \checkmark$$

This shows Ra plays the role of $Re^2\,Pr$ — it is the ratio of buoyancy inertia to the product of viscous and thermal damping. Churchill–Chu is the empirical fit that extends this result across all regimes.

10.5  The Thermal Expansion Coefficient $\beta$

For an ideal gas (which includes air and most processing gases):

$$\beta = \frac{1}{T_{\text{film}}} \quad [\text{K}^{-1}], \quad T \text{ in Kelvin}$$

This is one of the most important simplifications in natural convection. For air at a film temperature of 350 K, for example: $\beta = 1/350 = 2.86 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{K}^{-1}$.

For liquids (polymer melts, water, oil), $\beta$ must be looked up in property tables, since liquids are not ideal gases. Polymer melts typically have $\beta \approx 5\text{--}10 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{K}^{-1}$.

Common error: Using $T$ in °C instead of Kelvin when computing $\beta = 1/T_{\text{film}}$. Always convert to Kelvin first: $T[\text{K}] = T[\text{°C}] + 273.15$.

10.6  Churchill–Chu Correlation: Vertical Plate

The most widely used correlation for a vertical isothermal plate over the full range of $Ra$ is the Churchill–Chu (1975) equation:

$$\overline{Nu}_L = \left[0.825 + \frac{0.387\,Ra_L^{1/6}}{\left(1 + \left(\dfrac{0.492}{Pr}\right)^{9/16}\right)^{8/27}}\right]^2$$

Valid for: vertical plate or cylinder, $0 \le Ra_L \le 10^{12}$, any Pr.

The average heat-transfer coefficient follows from the definition of Nusselt number:

$$h = \frac{\overline{Nu}_L \cdot k_f}{L}$$

For air ($Pr \approx 0.71$) in the laminar range ($Ra \sim 10^5\text{--}10^9$), a simpler approximation works: $\overline{Nu}_L \approx 0.59\,Ra_L^{1/4}$ (laminar), $\overline{Nu}_L \approx 0.10\,Ra_L^{1/3}$ (turbulent). But Churchill–Chu covers all regimes in one formula.

Working with the Churchill–Chu formula: The inner fraction looks intimidating, but follow these steps: (1) compute $Ra^{1/6}$; (2) compute $(0.492/Pr)^{9/16}$; (3) add 1, raise to $8/27$; (4) divide into $0.387\,Ra^{1/6}$; (5) add 0.825; (6) square the result.

10.7  Horizontal Surfaces

For horizontal plates, the orientation of the hot or cold surface matters because buoyancy can either assist or oppose the flow.

Configuration Range of $Ra$ Correlation
Heated upper surface (or cooled lower surface) $10^4 \le Ra \le 10^7$ $Nu = 0.54\,Ra^{1/4}$
Heated upper surface (or cooled lower surface) $10^7 \le Ra \le 10^{11}$ $Nu = 0.15\,Ra^{1/3}$
Heated lower surface (or cooled upper surface) $10^5 \le Ra \le 10^{11}$ $Nu = 0.27\,Ra^{1/4}$

The characteristic length $L$ for horizontal plates is defined as $L = A_s / P$, where $A_s$ is the surface area and $P$ is the perimeter. For a $W \times D$ plate: $L = WD / [2(W+D)]$.

10.9  Worked Examples

Example 10.1 — Rayleigh Number for an Oven Wall

A vertical oven wall (height $L = 0.3\ \text{m}$) is at $T_s = 100\,°\text{C}$ in a room at $T_\infty = 20\,°\text{C}$. Using air properties at the film temperature ($\beta = 3.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{K}^{-1}$, $\nu = 1.5 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}$, $\alpha = 2.1 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}$), compute $Ra_L$.

Solution:

$\Delta T = 100 - 20 = 40\,°\text{C}$

$$Ra_L = \frac{g\,\beta\,\Delta T\,L^3}{\nu\,\alpha} = \frac{9.81 \times 3.0\times10^{-3} \times 40 \times (0.3)^3} {1.5\times10^{-5} \times 2.1\times10^{-5}}$$

Numerator: $9.81 \times 3.0\times10^{-3} \times 40 \times 0.027 = 9.81 \times 3.24\times10^{-3} = 0.03178$

Denominator: $1.5\times10^{-5} \times 2.1\times10^{-5} = 3.15\times10^{-10}$

$$Ra_L = \frac{0.03178}{3.15\times10^{-10}} \approx 1.01 \times 10^8$$

This is in the laminar range ($Ra < 10^9$). ✓

Example 10.2 — Churchill–Chu $Nu$ and $h$

For the oven wall in Example 10.1 ($Ra = 10^8$, $Pr = 0.714$, $k_{\text{air}} = 0.028\ \text{W/mK}$, $L = 0.3\ \text{m}$), find $\overline{Nu}_L$ and $h$ using Churchill–Chu.

Solution:

Step 1: $Ra^{1/6} = (10^8)^{1/6} = 10^{8/6} = 10^{1.333} \approx 21.54$

Step 2: $(0.492/0.714)^{9/16} = (0.689)^{0.5625} \approx 0.795$

Step 3: $(1 + 0.795)^{8/27} = 1.795^{0.296} \approx 1.156$

Step 4: $0.387 \times 21.54 / 1.156 \approx 7.21$

Step 5: $\overline{Nu} = (0.825 + 7.21)^2 = (8.035)^2 \approx 64.6$

(Slight rounding to $Ra = 10^8$ gives $Nu \approx 61$ with exact Ra.)

$$h = \frac{Nu \cdot k}{L} = \frac{61 \times 0.028}{0.3} = \frac{1.708}{0.3} \approx 5.7\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$$

This is a typical natural convection $h$ for air: modest, but significant for long cooling times.

10.10  Concept Questions

C1
Type 1

You double the height $L$ of a heated vertical plate while keeping $\Delta T$ constant. By what factor does $Ra_L$ change?

$Ra_L \propto L^3$, so doubling $L$ increases $Ra_L$ by $2^3 = 8\times$. This means $Nu \sim Ra^{1/4}$ increases by $8^{1/4} \approx 1.68\times$, and $h = Nu \cdot k/L$ changes by $1.68/2 \approx 0.84\times$ — actually decreasing slightly. Taller surfaces have lower average $h$ because the boundary layer grows thicker along its length.
C2
Type 1

Why is $\beta = 1/T_{\text{film}}$ only valid for ideal gases, and not for water or polymer melts?

Ideal gas law: $\rho = P/(RT)$, so $\beta = -(1/\rho)(\partial\rho/\partial T)_P = 1/T$. Liquids have a more complex equation of state; density depends on intermolecular forces and compressibility, not just temperature through $RT$. Their $\beta$ must be measured experimentally. Water near 4 °C even has $\beta < 0$!
C3
Type 2

A horizontal heated plate faces upward versus downward. Which orientation gives a higher $h$, and why?

Heated surface facing upward gives higher $h$. Warm air rises away from the surface freely, pulling cool air in. When the heated surface faces downward, warm air is trapped beneath it; gravity prevents efficient removal. The correlations reflect this: for heated-up $Nu = 0.54\,Ra^{1/4}$, while for heated-down $Nu = 0.27\,Ra^{1/4}$ — exactly half.
C4
Type 2

An engineer claims that adding insulation to the outside of a vertical polymer melt tank will slow natural convection cooling on the inside (melt side). Is this reasoning correct?

Partially correct but confused. Insulation reduces heat flow through the wall, so $T_s$ (inner wall temperature seen by the melt) rises closer to $T_{\text{melt}}$, reducing $\Delta T$ and hence $Ra$. A lower $Ra$ means weaker natural convection circulation in the melt — less mixing, more quiescent. The insulation does not directly slow convection; it reduces the driving temperature difference.
C5
Type 3 — Scaling Derivation

Derive the Rayleigh number from first principles using physical scaling.

(a) Consider a vertical plate with surface temperature $T_s$ in quiescent fluid at $T_\infty$. The buoyancy force per unit volume is $f_b = \rho g\beta\Delta T$. The viscous resistance force per unit volume scales as $f_v \sim \mu\,u / L^2$. Balance these to find the natural convection velocity scale $u_{\text{nc}}$ in terms of $g$, $\beta$, $\Delta T$, $L$, and $\nu$.

(b) Now form the thermal Péclet number $Pe = u_{\text{nc}} L / \alpha$ (ratio of convective to conductive heat transport). Substitute your $u_{\text{nc}}$ from part (a) and simplify. What familiar dimensionless group do you recover?

(c) From the scaling $Nu \sim Pe^{1/2}$ (thermal boundary-layer argument), write $Nu$ as a function of $Ra$. What exponent on $Ra$ do you expect for laminar natural convection, and does this match the Churchill–Chu simplified form $Nu \approx 0.59\,Ra^{1/4}$?

(d) A polymer melt pool has $g\beta\Delta T \approx 0.02\ \text{m/s}^2$, $L = 0.1\ \text{m}$, $\nu = 1\times10^{-4}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}$, $\alpha = 1\times10^{-7}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}$. Compute $Ra$ and $u_{\text{nc}}$. Is the flow laminar? Is convection significant ($Pe \gg 1$)?

(a) Velocity scale: $f_b = \rho g\beta\Delta T$, $f_v \sim \mu u/L^2 = \rho\nu u/L^2$. Setting $f_b = f_v$: $$u_{\text{nc}} \sim \frac{g\beta\Delta T\,L^2}{\nu}$$

(b) Péclet number: $$Pe = \frac{u_{\text{nc}}\,L}{\alpha} = \frac{g\beta\Delta T\,L^3}{\nu\,\alpha} = Ra_L \quad \checkmark$$ The Rayleigh number is the Péclet number for buoyancy-driven flow.

(c) Nu exponent: The thermal boundary layer thickness scales as $\delta_T \sim L\,Ra^{-1/4}$ (from the full boundary-layer energy balance), giving $Nu = hL/k \sim L/\delta_T \sim Ra^{1/4}$. This matches $Nu \approx 0.59\,Ra^{1/4}$ in the laminar range. ✓

(d) Polymer melt: $Ra = (0.02 \times (0.1)^3)/(10^{-4}\times10^{-7}) = 2\times10^{-5}/10^{-11} = 2\times10^6$ — laminar ($\ll 10^9$). $u_{\text{nc}} \sim 0.02\times0.01/10^{-4} = 2\times10^{-3}\ \text{m/s} = 2\ \text{mm/s}$. $Pe = Ra = 2\times10^6 \gg 1$ — convection strongly dominates conduction even in this viscous melt pool.

Key Ideas

  • Natural convection is driven entirely by buoyancy; no fan or pump is needed.
  • The Rayleigh number $Ra = Gr \cdot Pr$ replaces the Reynolds number as the governing parameter.
  • For an ideal gas, $\beta = 1/T_{\text{film}}$ (Kelvin) — memorize this.
  • The Churchill–Chu correlation covers laminar and turbulent regimes in one formula.
  • Typical $h$ for natural convection in air: $2\text{--}25\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$; for forced convection in air: $25\text{--}250\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$; for water: $500\text{--}10{,}000\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$.
  • Adding a fan can reduce cooling time by 5–20$\times$.
