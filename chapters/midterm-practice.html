✓ Answer Key — Midterm Practice Exam
Part A — Conceptual (Model Answers)
C1. Fourier’s law: heat flux is proportional to the temperature gradient; the constant of proportionality is $k$. High $k$ means heat moves easily. High-$k$ example: steel barrel ($k \approx 15$ W/m·K). Low-$k$ example: PLA, HDPE, foam insulation ($k \approx 0.04$–$0.44$ W/m·K).
C2. Newton’s law: $Q = h A \Delta T$. $h$ is an empirical coefficient capturing fluid motion, geometry, and fluid properties near the surface. To increase $h$: (1) increase fluid velocity (stronger fan/pump), (2) use a fluid with higher conductivity or lower viscosity.
C3. $q = \varepsilon \sigma (T_s^4 - T_\text{surr}^4)$. Radiation scales as $T^4$ while convection scales as $\Delta T$; at high $T_s$ the $T^4$ term grows rapidly and radiation dominates.
C4. $R_\text{wall}'' = L/k$; $R_\text{conv}'' = 1/h$. In series: $R_\text{total}'' = L/k + 1/h$; heat flux $q = \Delta T / R_\text{total}''$.
C5. For a cylinder, adding insulation increases the outer area for convection faster than it adds conductive resistance if $r < r_\text{cr} = k/h$. Adding insulation always reduces loss only when the insulation outer radius exceeds $r_\text{cr}$.
C6. The polymer liner ($R_1'' = 0.50$) dominates since it is > 90% of the total resistance (0.551 m²·K/W total). To extract heat faster, use a thinner polymer liner or replace it with a more conductive material. The steel layer is negligible.
C7. (a) Parabolic (concave-down) profile symmetric about the midplane. (b) $T_\text{max} = T_w + \dot{q}L^2/(2k)$, at the geometric midplane. (c) Doubling $k$ halves the temperature rise: $T_\text{max} - T_w$ is halved.
C8. (a) Melting of the crystalline fraction (first-order phase transition). (b) Heat of fusion $\Delta H_m$ (J/g). (c) Lower crystallinity or a broader distribution of crystal sizes/perfection.
C9. (a) $\alpha$ measures how quickly a material “conducts away” a temperature disturbance relative to its ability to store heat. (b) Polymers have low $k$ and high $c_p$ per unit mass, yielding low $\alpha$. (c) Longer cooling times per mm of wall thickness require either more cooling channels (closer spacing) or longer cycle times, increasing mold cost and production time.
C10. (a/b) Steady state with no heat generation: $d^2T/dx^2 = 0$, so $dT/dx = $ constant, meaning $T(x)$ is linear. Requires: steady state, constant $k$, 1D, no heat generation. (c) If $k$ increases with $T$, the governing equation becomes nonlinear: $d/dx[k(T)\,dT/dx] = 0$, so heat flux is still constant but $T(x)$ is curved (concave toward the hot side).
