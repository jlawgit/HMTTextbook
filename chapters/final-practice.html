✓ Answer Key — Final Exam Practice
Part A — Conceptual (Model Answers)
C1. Fourier: $q = -k\,dT/dx$ — driving force: temperature gradient; property: $k$; flux: $q$ (W/m2). Fick: $N = -D\,dC/dx$ — driving force: concentration gradient; property: $D$; flux: $N$ (mol/m2·s). Diffusivity $D$ (m2/s) plays the same role as $k$ (W/m·K). Both govern the proportionality between flux and gradient.
C2. $\text{Bi} = hL_c/k$. Bi $\ll$ 1: internal conduction resistance is negligible; the body temperature is nearly uniform at any instant (can use lumped capacitance). Criterion: Bi $\leq$ 0.1. When Bi is large, significant temperature gradients exist inside the body — a single "lump" temperature is no longer meaningful, and the spatial PDE must be solved.
C3. Nu = convective heat transfer / conductive heat transfer through the fluid layer. With $\text{Nu} \propto \text{Re}^{1/2}$, doubling Re multiplies Nu by $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41$. Since $h = \text{Nu}\cdot k/L$, $h$ also increases by factor $\sqrt{2}$.
C4. $\text{Ra} = g\beta\Delta T L^3/(\nu\alpha)$. Ra compares buoyancy-driven inertia to viscous and thermal diffusive damping. To increase Ra and hence $h$: (1) Increase $\Delta T$ — raises Ra directly; (2) Increase surface height $L$ — Ra $\propto L^3$; (3) Use a less viscous fluid or higher-conductivity fluid around the reactor — changes $\nu$ and $\alpha$.
C5. Free-volume theory: diffusion requires the molecule to “hop” into a free-volume void. As $T \to T_g$ from above, free volume decreases rapidly, so $D$ drops sharply (often by orders of magnitude). Small molecules need smaller voids than large molecules, so they find voids more often and diffuse faster.
C6. Film theory: concentration drops linearly across a stagnant layer of thickness $\delta$. $k_m = D/\delta$. Sh $= k_m L/D$ (mass analogue of Nu); Schmidt number Sc $= \nu/D$ is the analogue of Pr. Mass-transfer correlations have the form Sh $= f(\text{Re}, \text{Sc})$, mirroring Nu $= f(\text{Re}, \text{Pr})$.
C7. $P = D \times S$: diffusivity $D$ governs how fast a molecule moves through the membrane; solubility $S$ (mol/m3·Pa) governs how much dissolves. High $P$ with poor selectivity most likely reflects high $S$ for both gases (both dissolve readily) — the membrane is a good solvent for both. Or large $D$ (rubbery polymer) moves all molecules fast with little discrimination.
C8. (a) Large Fo: the body has exchanged a large multiple of its thermal energy capacity with the surroundings — it is close to equilibrium with $T_\infty$. (b) At $t = \tau$, $\theta^* = e^{-1} = 0.368$, so $T = 20 + 180 \times 0.368 = 86.2\,^\circ$C $\approx$ 86°C ✓. $\tau = \rho c_p V/(hA) = L_c/(h/(\rho c_p))$ also equals $\rho c_p L_c / h$. With $\tau = 100$ s and (assumed) $\rho c_p \approx 2 \times 10^6$ J/m3K: $h = \rho c_p L_c/\tau = 2\times10^6 \times 0.005/100 = 100$ W/m2K. Bi $= 100 \times 0.005/0.25 = 2$ — actually Bi $>$ 0.1, so this is illustrative; the exact calculation would require the full 1D transient solution.
C9. (a) Le $\gg 1$: thermal boundary layer is much thicker than the concentration boundary layer ($\delta_T/\delta_C \approx \text{Le}^{1/3}$ or Le, depending on regime). Heat diffuses much faster than mass. (b) Le $= 10^{-7}/10^{-9} = 100$. The thermal boundary layer is ∼100× thicker than the concentration boundary layer. (c) The polymer heats up quickly (thin concentration BL keeps solvent in), but solvent transport out is slow. The process is mass-transfer limited, not energy limited — hence slow drying despite efficient heating.
C10. (a) Rate-limiting processes: (i) heat supply to the film surface for evaporation (convective heat transfer from hot air), (ii) solvent diffusion out through the polymer matrix (mass transfer). To determine which limits: if increasing air temperature/velocity speeds up drying significantly, heat transfer is limiting; if it does not, mass transfer is limiting. (b) Lewis number Le $= \alpha/D = (0.15/(\rho c_p)) / (5\times10^{-10})$. Or compare $\tau_\text{thermal} = L^2/\alpha$ to $\tau_\text{diffusion} = L^2/D$: ratio $= \alpha/D = \text{Le}$. (c) Measure the solvent concentration profile through the film thickness (e.g., by FTIR-ATR at different depths) — a steep gradient near the surface confirms mass-transfer limitation.
