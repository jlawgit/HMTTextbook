Chapter 11

Molecular Diffusion

A molecule in solution does not sit still. Random thermal motion carries it through the surrounding medium one collision at a time. When many molecules do this together, the net result is a flux from high concentration to low. This chapter establishes Fick's two laws, introduces the diffusion coefficient, and shows how the same resistance framework used in heat conduction applies directly to mass transfer.

⏳ ~45 min 📐 3 worked examples 🧩 5 question pools 🔢 5 calculation problems

The Glove Problem

A chemical plant worker puts on nitrile rubber gloves before handling toluene. The gloves look intact. The container says the gloves are rated for toluene. But after two hours the worker's skin is irritated. What went wrong?

The glove did not tear. Toluene did not leak through a hole. Instead, individual toluene molecules diffused through the solid rubber, one molecular collision at a time, until enough had crossed to reach the skin. This is permeation: mass transfer through a solid membrane driven purely by a concentration gradient. No bulk flow, no pressure difference, just molecular random walks adding up to a net flux.

The same process governs oxygen transport through packaging films, drug release from polymer matrices, solvent uptake in coatings, and barrier performance in multilayer films. Every one of these problems is controlled by the diffusion coefficient $D$ and the concentration gradient across the membrane.

The question this chapter answers: what equation governs the diffusive flux, how does concentration evolve in time, and what physical properties control how fast a molecule moves through a polymer?

Figure 11.1: Steady-state diffusion through a polymer membrane. The concentration profile is linear when $D$ is constant. Net flux $J$ is directed from high to low concentration.

11.2 Fick's First Law

Adolf Fick observed in 1855 that the rate of mass transfer is proportional to the concentration gradient. For one-dimensional diffusion in the $x$ direction:

(11.1) $$J = -D\,\frac{dC}{dx}$$
$J$ = molar flux [mol/(m²·s)]  |  $D$ = diffusion coefficient [m²/s]  |  $C$ = molar concentration [mol/m³]

The minus sign has the same origin as Fourier's law: molecules move from high to low concentration, which is the direction of decreasing $C$, so the gradient $dC/dx$ is negative when flux is positive. The minus sign ensures $J > 0$ when mass moves in the positive $x$ direction.

Compare to Fourier's law: $q = -k\,dT/dx$ and $J = -D\,dC/dx$ are structurally identical. Temperature gradient drives heat flux; concentration gradient drives mass flux. The diffusion coefficient $D$ plays the same role as thermal conductivity $k$.

Steady-State Flux and Diffusive Resistance

For steady-state diffusion through a flat membrane of thickness $L$, the concentration profile is linear. Substituting the constant gradient gives:

(11.2) $$J = D\,\frac{C_1 - C_2}{L} = \frac{\Delta C}{R_\text{diff}}$$
$\Delta C = C_1 - C_2$ [mol/m³]  |  $R_\text{diff} = L/D$ = diffusive resistance [s/m]

The diffusive resistance $R = L/D$ increases with thickness and decreases with diffusivity. Two membranes placed in series add their resistances directly, just as thermal resistances do:

(11.3) $$R_\text{total} = \frac{L_1}{D_1} + \frac{L_2}{D_2}$$
Solubility in polymer membranes: The concentration $C$ in Fick's law refers to concentration inside the membrane, not in the adjacent fluid. For gases dissolving in polymers, $C = S \cdot p$ where $S$ is the solubility coefficient [mol/(m³·Pa)]. The combined quantity $P = D \cdot S$ is called permeability and is treated in Chapter 14.

Example 11.1

Steady-state O₂ permeation through an HDPE film

An HDPE packaging film (thickness $L = 0.50$ mm) separates oxygen at $C_1 = 2.0$ mol/m³ (outside) from a food package at $C_2 = 0$. The diffusion coefficient of O₂ in HDPE is $D = 2.0 \times 10^{-10}$ m²/s. Find the steady-state oxygen flux.

Solution

Convert thickness: $L = 5.0 \times 10^{-4}$ m. Apply Eq. 11.2:

$$J = D\,\frac{\Delta C}{L} = \frac{(2.0\times10^{-10})(2.0)}{5.0\times10^{-4}} = \frac{4.0\times10^{-10}}{5.0\times10^{-4}} = 8.0\times10^{-7}\;\mathrm{mol/(m^2{\cdot}s)}$$

This equals 0.80 μmol/(m²·s). For a 1 m² package, about 69 mmol/day of oxygen enters, more than enough to spoil fresh produce within days. A barrier coating that reduces $D$ by 100× extends shelf life proportionally.

11.3 Fick's Second Law

Fick's first law describes the flux at a given instant when the profile is known. To find how the profile itself evolves in time, apply a mass balance on a thin slab of thickness $\Delta x$. In the limit $\Delta x \to 0$:

(11.4) $$\frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D\,\frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial x^2}$$
Concentration changes in time because the local flux diverges: more molecules arrive than leave where $\partial^2 C/\partial x^2 > 0$.

This is identical in form to the heat conduction equation $\partial T/\partial t = \alpha\,\partial^2 T/\partial x^2$, with the thermal diffusivity $\alpha$ replaced by $D$. Every solution from transient heat conduction applies here with the substitution $\alpha \to D$ and $T \to C$.

Semi-Infinite Medium: the Error Function Solution

When a polymer slab is much thicker than the diffusion depth, the "semi-infinite" approximation applies. For a constant surface concentration $C_0$ imposed at $x = 0$ for $t > 0$, the solution to Eq. 11.4 is:

(11.5) $$C(x,t) = C_0\;\operatorname{erfc}\!\left(\frac{x}{2\sqrt{Dt}}\right)$$
$\operatorname{erfc}(u) = 1 - \operatorname{erf}(u) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}}\int_u^\infty e^{-s^2}\,ds$  |  $\operatorname{erfc}(0) = 1$, $\operatorname{erfc}(\infty) = 0$

At $x = 0$: $C = C_0$ for all $t > 0$ (the boundary condition). Far from the surface, $C \to 0$. As time increases, the profile shifts deeper into the material.

Diffusion Length and Fourier Number

The argument of erfc equals 1 when $x = 2\sqrt{Dt}$. At this depth the concentration has fallen to $\operatorname{erfc}(1) \approx 0.16\, C_0$. The diffusion length is:

(11.6) $$\delta_D = 2\sqrt{Dt}$$
Characteristic depth of concentration penetration after time $t$. Grows as $\sqrt{t}$, not linearly.

The Fourier number for mass transfer compares diffusion depth to a characteristic system length $L$:

(11.7) $$\mathrm{Fo}_m = \frac{Dt}{L^2}$$
$\mathrm{Fo}_m \ll 1$: diffusion has barely entered (semi-infinite valid).   $\mathrm{Fo}_m \sim 1$: front has reached the far boundary.
Scaling rule: Time to diffuse through thickness $L$ scales as $t \sim L^2/D$. Doubling thickness quadruples the time. This is why thick polymer coatings are highly effective diffusion barriers even when $D$ is only moderately small.

Figure 11.2: Concentration profiles $C(x,t) = C_0\,\operatorname{erfc}(x/2\sqrt{Dt})$ at three times. The surface is fixed at $C_0$. The diffusion front deepens as $\sqrt{t}$. The diffusion length $\delta_D$ (shown for $t_2$) marks the depth where $C \approx 0.16\,C_0$.

Example 11.2

Diffusion length in an epoxy coating after one hour

A thick epoxy coating is exposed to acetone vapor at $t = 0$. The diffusion coefficient of acetone in the epoxy is $D = 1.0 \times 10^{-11}$ m²/s. Estimate the diffusion length after 1 hour. Is the semi-infinite approximation valid for a 5 mm coating?

Solution

Convert time: $t = 3600$ s. Apply Eq. 11.6:

$$\delta_D = 2\sqrt{Dt} = 2\sqrt{(1.0\times10^{-11})(3600)} = 2\sqrt{3.6\times10^{-8}}$$ $$= 2\times1.90\times10^{-4}\;\mathrm{m} \approx 0.38\;\mathrm{mm}$$

After one hour the concentration front has penetrated only 0.38 mm out of a 5 mm coating. Check: $\mathrm{Fo}_m = Dt/L^2 = (10^{-11} \times 3600)/(5\times10^{-3})^2 = 0.0014 \ll 1$. The semi-infinite approximation is fully valid. The thick coating will not be saturated for many hours.

$\sqrt{t}$ scaling

To reach $\delta_D = 5$ mm (the full coating), solve $5\times10^{-3} = 2\sqrt{Dt}$: $t = (2.5\times10^{-3})^2/D = 6.25\times10^{-6}/10^{-11} = 6.25\times10^5$ s $\approx 7$ days. Sixteen times the depth requires 256 times the time, because of the $\sqrt{t}$ dependence.

11.4 The Diffusion Coefficient

The diffusion coefficient $D$ encodes how fast a given molecule moves through a given medium. Its value spans many orders of magnitude, depending on molecular size, medium viscosity, and temperature.

System Typical D (m²/s) Example
Small molecule in gas 10⁻⁵ to 10⁻⁴ O₂ in air
Small molecule in liquid 10⁻⁹ to 10⁻⁸ Toluene in water
Small molecule in polymer 10⁻¹⁴ to 10⁻¹⁰ O₂ in HDPE
Polymer chain in solution 10⁻¹⁸ to 10⁻¹⁶ PEG in water
Polymer in glassy polymer < 10⁻²⁰ PE chain in PMMA glass

The Stokes-Einstein Equation

For a spherical particle diffusing in a viscous solvent, Einstein (1905) combined Stokes drag with the equipartition theorem to obtain:

(11.8) $$D = \frac{k_B T}{6\pi\mu r}$$
$k_B = 1.38 \times 10^{-23}$ J/K  |  $\mu$ = dynamic viscosity [Pa·s]  |  $r$ = hydrodynamic radius of diffusing molecule [m]

Three key predictions follow directly. First, $D \propto 1/r$: larger molecules diffuse more slowly. A polymer chain with $r = 30$ nm diffuses 100 times more slowly than a solvent molecule with $r = 0.3$ nm in the same medium at the same temperature.

Second, $D \propto T/\mu$: raising temperature increases $D$ directly through the numerator and also decreases liquid viscosity $\mu$, so both effects raise $D$. This is why diffusion through rubbery polymers (above $T_g$) is orders of magnitude faster than through glassy polymers.

Third, $D \propto 1/\mu$: a molecule in glycerol ($\mu \approx 1.5$ Pa·s) diffuses about 1500 times more slowly than in water ($\mu \approx 0.001$ Pa·s).

Figure 11.3: The Stokes-Einstein equation predicts $D \propto 1/r$. A polymer chain with hydrodynamic radius 10 to 100 times larger than a small molecule diffuses 10 to 100 times more slowly in the same solvent at the same temperature.

Example 11.3

Stokes-Einstein comparison: solvent vs. dissolved polymer

A solvent molecule has hydrodynamic radius $r_s = 0.30$ nm. A dissolved polymer chain in the same solvent at the same temperature has hydrodynamic radius $r_p = 3.0$ nm. Find the ratio $D_s/D_p$.

Solution

From Eq. 11.8, $D = k_BT/(6\pi\mu r)$. Since $T$, $\mu$, $k_B$, and $6\pi$ are identical for both species:

$$\frac{D_s}{D_p} = \frac{r_p}{r_s} = \frac{3.0\;\text{nm}}{0.30\;\text{nm}} = \mathbf{10}$$

The solvent molecule diffuses 10 times faster than the polymer chain. In practical terms, solvent swelling fronts advance far ahead of any polymer redistribution, which is why solvent uptake kinetics are always governed by solvent diffusion, not polymer chain motion.

Chapter 11 Summary

Key Ideas

  • Fick's first law: $J = -D\,dC/dx$. Flux is proportional to concentration gradient, directed from high to low $C$. Structurally identical to Fourier's law.
  • Diffusive resistance: $R = L/D$. Layers in series add directly. The layer with the lowest $D$ controls the total resistance, even if thin.
  • Fick's second law: $\partial C/\partial t = D\,\partial^2 C/\partial x^2$. Governs transient diffusion; identical form to the heat equation with $\alpha \to D$.
  • Diffusion length: $\delta_D = 2\sqrt{Dt}$. Penetration advances as $\sqrt{t}$: doubling depth requires 4× the time.
  • Stokes-Einstein: $D = k_BT/(6\pi\mu r)$. Larger molecules diffuse more slowly ($D \propto 1/r$). Temperature and viscosity both matter.

Key Equations

  • Fick's first: $J = -D\,dC/dx$
  • Steady-state: $J = D(C_1-C_2)/L$
  • Resistance: $R = L/D$
  • Fick's second: $\partial C/\partial t = D\,\partial^2 C/\partial x^2$
  • Semi-infinite: $C = C_0\,\mathrm{erfc}(x/2\sqrt{Dt})$
  • Diffusion length: $\delta_D = 2\sqrt{Dt}$
  • Fourier number: $\mathrm{Fo}_m = Dt/L^2$
  • Stokes-Einstein: $D = k_BT/(6\pi\mu r)$
