Chapter 7

Thermal Characterisation: DSC, TGA, and TMA

The heat transfer calculations in Chapters 1–6 all required material properties: $k$, $\rho$, $c_p$, $T_g$, $T_m$, $\alpha$. This chapter shows where those numbers come from. Three instruments — the differential scanning calorimeter (DSC), the thermogravimetric analyser (TGA), and the thermomechanical analyser (TMA) — are the standard tools for measuring thermal properties of polymers. Every polymer scientist needs to be able to run them, read their output, and extract meaningful numbers.

✍
Cold Recall — Before You Read

Close your notes. Try each question from memory, then reveal the answer. The ones you cannot recall are exactly what this chapter reinforces.

RC-1. Write the formula for thermal diffusivity $\alpha$. Identify all three material properties it contains and explain physically what a high $\alpha$ means for how a polymer processes.

$$\alpha = \frac{k}{\rho\,c_p} \quad [\text{m}^2/\text{s}]$$ $k$ = thermal conductivity (W/mK); $\rho$ = density (kg/m³); $c_p$ = specific heat capacity (J/kgK). A high $\alpha$ means the material conducts heat quickly relative to how much it stores per unit volume. High-$\alpha$ polymers cool faster in a mold and heat up faster in an extruder. In Chapter 6, cooling time $\propto s^2/\alpha$, so $\alpha$ directly sets cycle time.

RC-2. From memory, give approximate $k$ values (W/mK) for PLA, HDPE, and steel. Which is lowest and why does that matter for polymer processing?

PLA $k \approx 0.13\ \text{W/mK}$;  HDPE $k \approx 0.44\ \text{W/mK}$;  Steel $k \approx 16\text{--}50\ \text{W/mK}$ (tool steel ∼35).

PLA has the lowest $k$ of the three. In processing, low $k$ means slow heat conduction through the polymer melt and slow cooling through the solidified part. Both effects increase cycle times. Low $k$ is why the polymer interior, not the mold wall or the coolant, so often controls cooling in injection molding.

RC-3. In Chapter 6, cooling time for an injection-molded part was proportional to $s^2/\alpha$. If a process engineer replaces a 2 mm HDPE wall with a 2 mm PLA wall, and $\alpha_{\text{HDPE}} = 4\,\alpha_{\text{PLA}}$, by what factor does cycle time change?

$t \propto s^2/\alpha$. Same $s$, but $\alpha_{\text{PLA}} = \alpha_{\text{HDPE}}/4$. Therefore $t_{\text{PLA}} = s^2/(\alpha_{\text{HDPE}}/4) = 4\,s^2/\alpha_{\text{HDPE}} = 4\,t_{\text{HDPE}}$. Cycle time quadruples. This is a severe productivity penalty that must be addressed by reducing wall thickness or accepting longer cycles.

7.1  Why Characterise?

You have been given a bag of white pellets labelled “polymer”. Before you can design a mold, set an extruder temperature, or predict how the part will behave in service, you need to know: When does it melt? How much heat does it release when crystallising? Does it absorb moisture? At what temperature does it soften under load? Does it contain fillers?

Thermal characterisation instruments answer all of these questions by measuring what happens to a small sample — typically 5–20 mg — as it is heated and cooled in a controlled way. The results feed directly into the heat transfer calculations you have been doing. DSC gives you $c_p$, $T_g$, $T_m$, and crystallinity. TGA tells you thermal stability and filler content. TMA reveals how dimensions change with temperature. Together, they form the thermal fingerprint of a polymer.

Pattern Check

Before reading on — the central equation of this chapter is $c_p = \dot{Q}\,/\,(m\,\beta)$, which is just a rearrangement of $\dot{Q} = m\,c_p\,\beta$ — the same heat-capacity formula you used in every energy balance in Chapters 5 and 6. DSC is simply a measuring instrument for $c_p$, $T_m$, and $\Delta H_m$; the underlying physics has not changed. Similarly, the crystallinity formula $X_c = \Delta H_m/\Delta H_m^\circ$ is a ratio of "heat released by your sample" to "heat released by a perfect crystal" — the same fraction structure as percent yield in chemistry. Once you have $c_p$ from DSC, it feeds directly into $\alpha = k/(\rho\,c_p)$, which feeds into the Ballman–Shusman cooling-time formula from Chapter 6. The chain is: DSC → $c_p$ → $\alpha$ → $t_c$.

7.2  DSC: Differential Scanning Calorimetry

A DSC measures the heat flow into or out of a sample relative to an inert reference as both are heated or cooled at a controlled rate $\beta$ (K/min). The instrument records $dQ/dt$ (mW) vs. temperature (or time).

The governing relationship between heat flow and specific heat is:

$$\frac{dQ}{dt} = m\,c_p\,\frac{dT}{dt} = m\,c_p\,\beta$$

Rearranging, the specific heat can be extracted directly from the measured heat flow:

$$c_p = \frac{dQ/dt}{m\,\beta}$$

where $m$ is the sample mass (kg) and $\beta = dT/dt$ is the heating rate (K/s). In practice, DSC software integrates the heat flow curve over a temperature range to give the total enthalpy change $\Delta H$ (J/g):

$$\Delta H = \frac{1}{m}\int_{T_1}^{T_2} \frac{dQ}{dt}\,dt$$

Each thermal event in the polymer — glass transition, crystallisation, melting — produces a characteristic signature in the heat flow curve.

Measurement equation

$$c_p = \frac{\dot{Q}}{m\,\beta}, \quad \Delta H = \frac{1}{m}\int \dot{Q}\,dt$$

DSC measures heat flow $\dot{Q}$ (mW) vs. temperature; dividing by mass and scan rate extracts $c_p$; integrating a peak gives $\Delta H$.

Process chain scaling

$$c_p \xrightarrow{\,\div\,} \alpha = \frac{k}{\rho\,c_p} \xrightarrow{\,\propto\,} t_c \propto \frac{s^2}{\alpha}$$

Higher $c_p$ → lower $\alpha$ → longer cooling time. One DSC measurement unlocks the entire injection-molding thermal calculation.

Step 1 — DSC extracts $c_p$. From the measured heat flow at the baseline (away from any peaks): $c_p = \dot{Q}/(m\beta)$. Typical polymer values: 1400–2400 J/kgK.

Step 2 — Combine to get diffusivity $\alpha$. Using conductivity $k$ from a flash diffusivity or guarded hot plate measurement and density $\rho$ from pycnometry: $\alpha = k/(\rho\,c_p)$. Since $c_p$ appears in the denominator, a polymer with a high $c_p$ (like nylon) has a low $\alpha$ — it stores heat effectively and releases it slowly.

Step 3 — Predict cooling time. $t_c \propto s^2/\alpha$: a material with $\alpha = 0.6\times10^{-7}$ (PLA) cools $2.3/0.6 \approx 3.8\times$ more slowly than HDPE ($\alpha = 2.3\times10^{-7}$) for the same wall thickness. The DSC $c_p$ number is not abstract — it directly sets your cycle time.
Sign convention: Most DSC instruments plot exothermic events (heat released by the sample, e.g., crystallisation) as downward peaks and endothermic events (heat absorbed, e.g., melting) as upward peaks. Some older instruments use the reverse. Always check the axis label.

7.3  Reading a DSC Curve

A first-heating DSC scan of a semi-crystalline polymer typically shows three events:

Glass transition ($T_g$): A step change in baseline heat flow (no peak). The polymer chains gain mobility at $T_g$; the specific heat $c_p$ jumps upward. $T_g$ is read as the midpoint of the step. Below $T_g$: glassy, stiff. Above $T_g$: rubbery (amorphous regions) or still solid (crystalline regions).

Cold crystallisation ($T_{cc}$): An exothermic peak on heating, appearing only if the sample was quenched (cooled rapidly) from the melt. Rapid cooling trapped the chains in an amorphous state; on reheating they have enough mobility to organise into crystals, releasing heat. Cold crystallisation means the sample was under-crystallised during the original cooling.

Melting ($T_m$): A large endothermic peak. The crystalline regions absorb heat to disorder their chains. The peak area equals the melting enthalpy $\Delta H_m$ (J/g), which is used to calculate crystallinity.

Feature Thermal event Direction What you extract
Baseline step Glass transition $T_g$ Step up in heat flow $T_g$ (midpoint), $\Delta c_p$
Exothermic peak Cold crystallisation $T_{cc}$ Downward peak $T_{cc}$, $\Delta H_{cc}$ (J/g)
Endothermic peak Melting $T_m$ Upward peak $T_m$ (peak), $\Delta H_m$ (J/g)
Endothermic baseline rise Dehydration / moisture loss Broad upward feature <150 °C Moisture content (wt%)
First heat vs. second heat: The first heating scan reflects the thermal history of the sample (how it was processed, stored, cooled). The second heating scan (after controlled cooling in the DSC) gives the “true” equilibrium properties, free from processing history. Always report which scan you are showing.

7.4  Degree of Crystallinity from DSC

The degree of crystallinity $X_c$ (dimensionless or %) is calculated from the ratio of the measured melting enthalpy to the reference value for a 100% crystalline sample of the same polymer:

$$X_c = \frac{\Delta H_m - \Delta H_{cc}}{\Delta H_m^\circ} \times 100\%$$

where $\Delta H_m$ is the measured melting peak area (J/g), $\Delta H_{cc}$ is the cold crystallisation peak area (J/g, subtracted because this heat was generated during the DSC scan, not stored from processing), and $\Delta H_m^\circ$ is the reference enthalpy for a perfectly crystalline sample. If no cold crystallisation peak appears, the formula reduces to $X_c = \Delta H_m / \Delta H_m^\circ$.

Polymer $\Delta H_m^\circ$ (J/g) Typical $X_c$ range (%)
HDPE29360–80
LDPE29330–50
Nylon 624025–50
Nylon 6/619625–50
PLA930–45
PET14020–60
ABS—0 (amorphous)

Crystallinity directly affects thermal conductivity ($k$ increases with $X_c$ because crystalline lamellae conduct better than amorphous regions), density, stiffness, and the heat per shot in injection molding (more crystallinity = more latent heat to remove).

7.5  TGA: Thermogravimetric Analysis

A TGA measures sample mass as a function of temperature (or time at fixed temperature). The sample sits on a precision balance inside a furnace; the instrument records $m(T)$ in milligrams or as a percentage of the initial mass.

Three types of mass loss appear in polymer TGA curves:

Moisture / solvent loss (25–150 °C): Small, early mass loss. Nylons absorb 2–3% moisture; this appears as a broad initial dip. The weight percent moisture equals the mass loss in this region divided by the initial mass.

Decomposition / pyrolysis (300–550 °C for most polymers): The main mass loss event. Polymer backbone scission and volatilisation. The onset temperature $T_{\text{onset}}$ (the temperature at which mass loss begins to accelerate) is a standard thermal stability metric. In air vs. nitrogen, oxidative degradation shifts $T_{\text{onset}}$ lower in air.

Residue at 600 °C: Non-combustible inorganic filler (glass fibre, carbon black, mineral filler, ash) remains after full pyrolysis. The residue weight percent equals the filler or ash content:

$$\text{Filler wt\%} = \frac{m_{\text{residue}}}{m_{\text{initial}}} \times 100\%$$

Atmosphere matters: Run TGA in nitrogen (inert) to measure pyrolysis onset without oxidation. Run in air to test oxidative stability or to cleanly combust organic content and reveal inorganic residue. Always report the gas used.

HDPE450–490<0.1% (no filler)
Nylon 6/6410–450<1%
PLA330–360<0.1%

7.6  TMA: Thermomechanical Analysis

A TMA measures the change in sample dimension (length or thickness) as a function of temperature under a small, constant applied force. The primary output is the coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE):

$$\alpha_L = \frac{1}{L_0}\frac{dL}{dT} \quad [\text{K}^{-1}\text{ or ppm/K}]$$

Below $T_g$, the glassy polymer has a relatively low, constant CTE. At $T_g$, there is a marked change in slope as the rubbery regions become mobile: the CTE increases significantly. This inflection point is an independent, load-based measurement of $T_g$ that complements the DSC heat flow measurement.

TMA also reveals creep (dimensional change under constant load over time) and softening (the temperature at which the probe penetrates the sample, marking the practical upper use temperature under that specific stress).

Polymer CTE below $T_g$ (ppm/K) CTE above $T_g$ (ppm/K)
HDPE100–200200–400
Nylon 6/670–100140–200
ABS70–90150–200
PLA68–80100–150
Steel (comparison)10–13—

The large mismatch between polymer CTE (~100 ppm/K) and metal CTE (~10 ppm/K) is why polymer parts bonded to metal substrates are prone to thermally induced stress and delamination when cycled through temperature changes.

7.7  Connecting Characterisation to Heat Transfer Calculations

Every DSC, TGA, and TMA measurement feeds directly into the heat transfer parameters used throughout this course:

Characterisation result Where it feeds into heat transfer
$c_p$ from DSC baseline $\alpha = k/(\rho c_p)$; melt energy balance $\dot{Q} = \dot{m}\,c_p\,\Delta T$; cooling time $t \propto s^2/\alpha$
$\Delta H_m$ (melting enthalpy) Latent heat term in injection molding shot heat: $Q = m(c_p\Delta T + H_c)$
$T_g$ and $T_m$ Set processing window (above $T_m$ for semi-crystalline; above $T_g$ for amorphous). Set ejection temperature in Chapter 6.
$X_c$ (crystallinity) Affects $k$ (higher $X_c \rightarrow$ higher $k$), density $\rho$, and latent heat per gram.
$T_{\text{onset}}$ from TGA Sets the upper temperature limit for processing; above $T_{\text{onset}}$, the polymer degrades irreversibly.
Filler content from TGA residue Filled polymers have higher $k$, $\rho$, and $\alpha$ than unfilled. Must use composite property values in calculations.
CTE from TMA Thermal stress in bonded or constrained components: $\sigma = E\,\alpha_L\,\Delta T$.

7.9  Worked Examples

Example 7.1 — Thermal Diffusivity from DSC Data

A DSC experiment on a polymer film gives $c_p = 2000\ \text{J/kgK}$. A separate hot-disk measurement gives $k = 0.40\ \text{W/mK}$, and the density is $\rho = 1000\ \text{kg/m}^3$. Compute the thermal diffusivity and state its significance for mold cooling.

Solution:

$$\alpha = \frac{k}{\rho\,c_p} = \frac{0.40}{1000 \times 2000} = 2.0 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}$$

From Chapter 6, cooling time $t_c \propto s^2/\alpha$. For a 2 mm wall ($s = 1\ \text{mm}$): $s^2/\alpha = (10^{-3})^2/(2.0\times10^{-7}) = 5\ \text{s}$. This polymer would require several seconds of mold cooling even with ideal mold contact.

Example 7.2 — Crystallinity and Latent Heat Correction

A nylon DSC curve shows a melting peak with $\Delta H_m = 120\ \text{J/g}$ and no cold crystallisation peak. The reference value for 100% crystalline nylon is $\Delta H_m^\circ = 240\ \text{J/g}$. Find $X_c$ and the latent heat contribution to the shot heat for a 0.10 kg part.

Solution:

$$X_c = \frac{120}{240} \times 100 = 50\%$$

Latent heat per gram: $\Delta H_m = 120\ \text{J/g} = 120{,}000\ \text{J/kg}$. For 0.10 kg: $Q_{\text{latent}} = 0.10 \times 120{,}000 = 12{,}000\ \text{J} = 12\ \text{kJ}$. This is additional to the sensible heat and must be removed by the mold cooling system.

C5
Hard

From a DSC report to a cooling-time prediction.

A DSC report on an unknown semi-crystalline polymer gives: $c_p = 1800\ \text{J/kgK}$ (melt), $T_m = 180\,°\text{C}$, $T_g = 55\,°\text{C}$, $\Delta H_m = 60\ \text{J/g}$, $\Delta H_m^\circ = 93\ \text{J/g}$. A separate measurement gives $k = 0.16\ \text{W/mK}$, $\rho = 1240\ \text{kg/m}^3$.

(a) Identify whether this polymer is likely PLA, nylon, or HDPE, using the values of $T_g$, $T_m$, and $\Delta H_m^\circ$ as clues. Calculate the degree of crystallinity $X_c$.

(b) Calculate the thermal diffusivity $\alpha$ from the DSC $c_p$ and the given $k$, $\rho$. Express in m²/s.

(c) The polymer is injection-molded into a part with half-wall thickness $s = 1.5\ \text{mm}$. Mold temperature $T_w = 30\,°\text{C}$, ejection temperature $T_e = 70\,°\text{C}$. Use the Ballman–Shusman formula $$t_c = \frac{s^2}{\pi^2\alpha}\ln\!\left(\frac{4}{\pi}\cdot\frac{T_m-T_w}{T_e-T_w}\right)$$ to calculate $t_c$ (s).

(d) A competitor material has $c_p = 2400\ \text{J/kgK}$ and the same $k$ and $\rho$. Without calculating, predict whether its $t_c$ will be longer or shorter, and by roughly what factor. Explain the physical reason.

(a) Polymer identification & crystallinity. Clues: $T_g \approx 55\,°\text{C}$ (nylon 6 has $T_g \approx 47$–60 °C; PLA $\approx 55$–60 °C), $T_m = 180\,°\text{C}$ (PLA$\approx$170–180 °C; nylon 6 = 220 °C), $\Delta H_m^\circ = 93\ \text{J/g}$ (literature value for PLA). Most likely PLA.
$$X_c = \frac{\Delta H_m}{\Delta H_m^\circ} \times 100 = \frac{60}{93} \times 100 \approx \mathbf{64.5\%}$$ (Note: this is unusually high for PLA and would imply a slow-cooled, well-annealed sample.)

(b) Thermal diffusivity. $$\alpha = \frac{k}{\rho\,c_p} = \frac{0.16}{1240 \times 1800} = \frac{0.16}{2{,}232{,}000} \approx \mathbf{7.17 \times 10^{-8}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}}$$ This is consistent with PLA ($\alpha \approx 6$–$8\times10^{-8}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}$).

(c) Cooling time. Convert: $s = 1.5 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}$. $$\frac{T_m - T_w}{T_e - T_w} = \frac{180-30}{70-30} = \frac{150}{40} = 3.75$$ $$\ln\!\left(\frac{4}{\pi} \times 3.75\right) = \ln(4.775) \approx 1.563$$ $$t_c = \frac{(1.5\times10^{-3})^2}{\pi^2 \times 7.17\times10^{-8}} \times 1.563 = \frac{2.25\times10^{-6}}{7.076\times10^{-7}} \times 1.563 \approx 3.18 \times 1.563 \approx \mathbf{4.97\ \text{s}} \approx 5\ \text{s}$$
(d) Effect of higher $c_p$. Higher $c_p = 2400$ vs. 1800 J/kgK means: $$\alpha' = \frac{0.16}{1240 \times 2400} = \frac{0.16}{2{,}976{,}000} \approx 5.38\times10^{-8}$$ Since $t_c \propto 1/\alpha \propto c_p$, the ratio is $t_c'/t_c = \alpha/\alpha' = 7.17/5.38 \approx 1.33\times$. The competitor material cools about 33% slower. Physical reason: higher $c_p$ means the polymer stores more thermal energy per degree, so a given heat removal rate takes longer to cool it to ejection temperature.

B1
Bridge → Lab

In your polymer characterisation lab, you receive an opaque white film labelled only “Sample X.” A DSC first-heating scan gives the following features:

  • A broad moisture endotherm from 50–100 °C (mass loss confirmed by TGA: 2.5%)
  • A glass transition step at $T_g = 47\,°\text{C}$
  • A cold crystallisation exotherm at $T_{cc} = 95\,°\text{C}$ with $\Delta H_{cc} = 22\ \text{J/g}$
  • A melting endotherm at $T_m = 178\,°\text{C}$ with $\Delta H_m = 44\ \text{J/g}$ (peak area)

The TGA in nitrogen shows $T_{\text{onset}} = 340\,°\text{C}$ and essentially zero residue at 600 °C.

(a) Is Sample X amorphous or semi-crystalline? Justify.

(b) Calculate the degree of crystallinity using $\Delta H_m^\circ = 93\ \text{J/g}$.

(c) Based on $T_g \approx 47\,°\text{C}$, $T_m \approx 178\,°\text{C}$, the presence of crystallinity, and the moisture sensitivity, identify the most likely polymer. Justify each clue.

(d) The film will be injection-molded. What drying protocol do you recommend before processing, and what maximum barrel temperature would you set?

(a) Semi-crystalline. The DSC shows both a melting peak ($T_m$) and a cold crystallisation peak, both of which require the presence of crystallisable polymer chains. An amorphous polymer would show only $T_g$.

(b) Degree of crystallinity: $$X_c = \frac{\Delta H_m - \Delta H_{cc}}{\Delta H_m^\circ} = \frac{44 - 22}{93} = \frac{22}{93} = 23.7\%\ \approx 24\%$$ The large cold crystallisation peak (half the melting area) tells us the sample was heavily quenched — much of the crystallinity formed during the DSC scan, not during the original processing.

(c) Most likely polymer: PLA (polylactic acid). Clue-by-clue:
• $T_g \approx 47\text{--}60\,°\text{C}$: matches PLA (55–60 °C for PLLA). Close match. ✓
• $T_m \approx 178\,°\text{C}$: characteristic of PLLA. ✓
• $\Delta H_m^\circ = 93\ \text{J/g}$: the provided reference value is exactly the literature value for PLA. ✓
• Moisture sensitivity: PLA is hygroscopic, absorbing moisture that causes hydrolytic degradation during melt processing. ✓
• Zero TGA residue: PLA is fully bio-based and leaves no inorganic ash. ✓
• $T_{\text{onset}} = 340\,°\text{C}$: consistent with PLA in nitrogen. ✓

(d) Processing recommendations:
Drying: 80 °C in a desiccant dryer for at least 4 hours to reduce moisture below 0.025% (250 ppm). The TGA shows 2.5% moisture — 100$\times$ above the processing limit. Without drying, the water hydrolyses PLA ester bonds during melt processing, drastically reducing molecular weight and giving brittle, low-strength parts.
Maximum barrel temperature: 210 °C (taking a safety margin below $T_m + 30\,°\text{C}$ and well below $T_{\text{onset}} = 340\,°\text{C}$). Typical PLA injection molding: 180–210 °C. Residence time in the barrel should be minimised (no stagnation zones) to prevent thermal degradation.

7.12  Chapter Summary

Key Equations

Quantity Formula Source
Specific heat from DSC $c_p = Q\,/\,(m\,\Delta T)$ DSC enthalpy integration
Heat flow at constant rate $dQ/dt = m\,c_p\,\beta$ DSC baseline region
Degree of crystallinity $X_c = (\Delta H_m - \Delta H_{cc})\,/\,\Delta H_m^\circ \times 100\%$ DSC melting peak
Filler / ash content Filler wt% $= m_{\text{residue}}/m_{\text{initial}} \times 100\%$ TGA at 600 °C in air

Key Ideas

  • DSC gives $T_g$, $T_m$, $c_p$, $\Delta H_m$, and crystallinity. It is the most information-dense thermal characterisation tool for polymers.
  • The degree of crystallinity $X_c = \Delta H_m / \Delta H_m^\circ$ sets latent heat per gram, thermal conductivity, and density.
  • A cold crystallisation peak means the sample was quenched; the true processing crystallinity uses $(\Delta H_m - \Delta H_{cc})$.
  • TGA reveals thermal stability ($T_{\text{onset}}$), moisture content (loss <150 °C), and filler/ash fraction (residue at 600 °C).
  • TMA measures CTE and gives a load-based $T_g$ that complements the DSC value.
  • All three instruments feed directly into heat transfer calculations: $c_p \rightarrow \alpha$; $\Delta H_m \rightarrow Q_{\text{shot}}$; $T_g$, $T_m \rightarrow$ processing window and ejection temperature.
