Chapter 12

Diffusion in Polymers

In small-molecule fluids, diffusion is fast and nearly temperature-independent. In polymers, it is orders of magnitude slower, exquisitely sensitive to temperature, and sometimes follows rules unlike anything Fick described. This chapter explains why, and shows how that difference is exploited in pharmaceutical patches, food packaging, and membrane separations.

📖 ~45 min read 📝 8 equations 🧠 3 worked examples 🔎 5 concept checks
Why This Chapter Matters

The Nicotine Patch

A nicotine replacement patch is a laminate of polymer films, each a fraction of a millimeter thick. The backing layer is nearly impermeable; the rate-controlling membrane releases active ingredient into the skin at a precisely engineered dose over 24 hours. The difference between these two layers is not chemistry but physics: one polymer sits above its glass transition temperature (rubbery, mobile chains, fast diffusion), the other below it (glassy, frozen free volume, slow diffusion). The ratio of diffusion coefficients across the laminate spans six orders of magnitude, all because of one transition in polymer chain mobility.

Every food package, gas-separation membrane, and protective coating works on the same principle. This chapter gives you the equations to design them.

Before you read: A rubbery polymer and a glassy polymer of the same chemical structure have diffusion coefficients that differ by a factor of $10^4$. What structural feature changes at $T_g$ to cause this?

12.1 Why Polymers Are Different

In Chapter 11, the diffusion coefficient $D$ was treated as a material constant. In low-molecular-weight liquids that is a fair approximation. In polymers, $D$ can vary by ten orders of magnitude depending on:

  • Temperature relative to the glass transition $T_g$
  • Degree of crystallinity (crystalline lamellae are impenetrable)
  • Penetrant size and shape
  • Concentration of absorbed penetrant (plasticization)

The central insight is that a small molecule diffuses by jumping into free volume: the unoccupied space between polymer chains. In a rubbery polymer above $T_g$, chain segments are mobile and redistribute free volume continuously, providing frequent jump opportunities. Below $T_g$, the chain segments are frozen, free volume is fixed and scarce, and diffusion is orders of magnitude slower.

Crystallinity Effects

Polymer crystals are close-packed and essentially impenetrable to small molecules. A penetrant must navigate around crystalline lamellae, taking a tortuous path through the amorphous regions only. The effective diffusion coefficient in a semicrystalline polymer is:

(12.1) $$D_{\rm eff} = \frac{D_{\rm am}}{\tau \cdot \phi_{\rm am}^{-1}}$$
$D_{\rm am}$ is the diffusion coefficient in the purely amorphous phase; $\tau$ is the tortuosity factor (how much longer the actual path is versus straight-line); $\phi_{\rm am}$ is the amorphous volume fraction. Both $\tau$ and $\phi_{\rm am}$ depend on crystallinity, so $D_{\rm eff}$ can drop by one to two orders of magnitude at 50% crystallinity.
Tortuosity in practice. For oriented semicrystalline films (like drawn PET bottles), the crystalline platelets are aligned perpendicular to the film surface, maximizing tortuosity in the transport direction. This is why stretch-blown PET has much better barrier properties than unoriented sheet of the same composition.

Figure 12.1: Tortuous diffusion path through a semicrystalline polymer. Crystalline lamellae (blue) act as impermeable baffles; the penetrant (red dashed path) must navigate around each baffle through the gap on the opposite edge, traveling far more than the direct straight-line distance (gray). Tortuosity $\tau$ quantifies this path lengthening, and oriented lamellae (as in drawn PET) maximize $\tau$ to minimize permeability.

12.2 Temperature Dependence: The Arrhenius Equation

For a rubbery polymer in a moderate temperature range above $T_g$, the diffusion coefficient follows an Arrhenius (activated-rate) form:

(12.2) $$D = D_0 \exp\!\left(-\frac{E_a}{RT}\right)$$
$D_0$ [m²/s] is the pre-exponential factor (a material constant); $E_a$ [J/mol] is the activation energy for diffusion; $R = 8.314$ J/(mol·K); $T$ [K] is absolute temperature. A large $E_a$ means $D$ is extremely sensitive to temperature.

Activation Energy and the Arrhenius Plot

Taking the ratio at two temperatures eliminates $D_0$ and yields a practical working form:

(12.3) $$\ln\!\frac{D_2}{D_1} = \frac{E_a}{R}\!\left(\frac{1}{T_1} - \frac{1}{T_2}\right)$$
This form is used whenever $D$ at one temperature is known and $D$ at another temperature is required, or when $E_a$ is to be measured from two data points. On an Arrhenius plot ($\ln D$ vs. $1/T$), the slope equals $-E_a/R$.

Activation energies for diffusion in rubbery polymers are typically 20 to 80 kJ/mol, increasing with penetrant size. A helium atom ($E_a \approx 20$ kJ/mol) is much less sensitive to temperature than an organic molecule like toluene ($E_a \approx 60$ kJ/mol). This has practical consequences for gas-separation membranes, where operating temperature can tune selectivity.

Figure 12.2: Arrhenius plot of $\ln D$ versus $1/T$ for diffusion in a rubbery polymer (blue solid, gentle slope, low $E_a$) and a glassy polymer (red dashed). Above $T_g$ the glassy polymer behaves like a rubbery material (same slope); below $T_g$ the slope steepens sharply because frozen free volume raises the effective activation energy. The slope of each segment equals $-E_a/R$.

Example 12.1

Activation energy from two-temperature data

The diffusion coefficient of CO₂ in a rubbery silicone membrane is $D_1 = 1.0 \times 10^{-12}$ m²/s at $T_1 = 300$ K and $D_2 = 1.0 \times 10^{-11}$ m²/s at $T_2 = 350$ K. Find the activation energy $E_a$.

Solution

Rearrange Eq. 12.3 for $E_a$:

$$E_a = R\,\frac{\ln(D_2/D_1)}{(1/T_1 - 1/T_2)}$$

Compute each term:

$$\ln\!\frac{D_2}{D_1} = \ln(10) = 2.303$$ $$\frac{1}{T_1} - \frac{1}{T_2} = \frac{1}{300} - \frac{1}{350} = \frac{50}{300\times350} = 4.76\times10^{-4}\;\mathrm{K^{-1}}$$ $$E_a = (8.314)\,\frac{2.303}{4.76\times10^{-4}} = \frac{19.14}{4.76\times10^{-4}} \approx 40{,}200\;\mathrm{J/mol}$$

The activation energy is 40 kJ/mol, typical for a small gas molecule (CO₂) in a rubbery polymer. A tenfold increase in $D$ results from raising temperature by only 50 K, illustrating why warm storage dramatically accelerates gas permeation through elastomers.

12.3 Free-Volume Theory

The Arrhenius equation describes the temperature dependence once the mechanism is established. Free-volume theory explains why the mechanism changes so dramatically at $T_g$ and how diffusion depends on polymer structure.

The fractional free volume $v_f$ is the fraction of total volume not occupied by polymer chains: $v_f = (V - V_0)/V$, where $V$ is the specific volume and $V_0$ is the volume of the chain segments themselves. Doolittle (and later Cohen and Turnbull) showed that diffusion requires a free-volume fluctuation large enough to accommodate the penetrant, leading to:

(12.4) $$D = A\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{B}{v_f}\right)$$
$A$ [m²/s] and $B$ (dimensionless) are material constants; $v_f$ is the fractional free volume. Because $v_f$ is small (typically 0.02 to 0.15), small changes in $v_f$ cause large changes in $D$. This is the equation of a drug-delivery membrane engineer: change the polymer architecture to tune $v_f$ and you tune the release rate.

The Glass Transition Jump

Below $T_g$ the polymer is in a non-equilibrium glassy state: free volume is "frozen in" at its $T_g$ value and increases only slowly on cooling. Above $T_g$ the polymer is in thermodynamic equilibrium (rubbery): free volume increases linearly with temperature at rate $\Delta\alpha = \alpha_{\rm rubber} - \alpha_{\rm glass}$ (the difference in thermal expansion coefficients). This creates a sharp change in the slope of $v_f(T)$ at $T_g$, which appears as a kink in the Arrhenius plot of $\ln D$ vs. $1/T$.

Design rule for barrier coatings. To minimize gas permeability, choose a polymer with (a) high $T_g$ so the coating is glassy at service temperature, (b) high crystallinity to reduce the amorphous volume fraction, and (c) rigid backbone and bulky side groups to reduce $v_f$ in the glassy state. EVOH, PVDC, and nylon MXD6 satisfy all three criteria, which is why they appear in multilayer food packaging.
Concentration-dependent diffusion. When a penetrant is absorbed in large quantities (e.g., water in a hydrophilic polymer), it acts as a plasticizer, reducing $T_g$ and increasing $v_f$ locally. This makes $D$ concentration-dependent: $D$ increases as the concentration front advances, so the actual flux is higher than a constant-$D$ calculation would predict. Chapter 11 solutions assume constant $D$; check this assumption whenever swelling is visible.

12.4 Permeability and Barrier Design

The steady-state flux of a gas through a polymer membrane depends on how easily it dissolves (solubility $S$) and how fast it moves once dissolved (diffusivity $D$). The combination is the permeability:

(12.5) $$P = D \cdot S$$
$P$ [mol/(m·s·Pa)] is the permeability; $D$ [m²/s] is the diffusion coefficient; $S$ [mol/(m³·Pa)] is the solubility (Henry-law coefficient). Note that $P$ combines a kinetic factor ($D$) and a thermodynamic factor ($S$), so two polymers with the same $P$ may have very different $D$ and $S$ individually.

The steady-state flux through a film of thickness $L$ under pressure difference $\Delta P$ is:

(12.6) $$J = \frac{P\,\Delta P}{L}$$
$J$ [mol/(m²·s)] is the molar flux; $\Delta P$ [Pa] is the partial-pressure difference across the film. This is the mass-transfer analogue of Fourier's law: flux is proportional to driving force and inversely proportional to thickness.
Example 12.2

CO₂ loss through a PET bottle wall

A PET bottle wall has thickness $L = 0.50$ mm. The diffusion coefficient of CO₂ in PET is $D = 2.0\times10^{-12}$ m²/s and the solubility is $S = 2.5\times10^{-3}$ mol/(m³·Pa). The carbonation pressure inside is $\Delta P = 1.0\times10^5$ Pa above ambient. Find the steady-state CO₂ flux.

Solution

Compute permeability using Eq. 12.5:

$$P = D\cdot S = (2.0\times10^{-12})(2.5\times10^{-3}) = 5.0\times10^{-15}\;\mathrm{mol/(m{\cdot}s{\cdot}Pa)}$$

Apply Eq. 12.6 with $L = 5.0\times10^{-4}$ m:

$$J = \frac{P\,\Delta P}{L} = \frac{(5.0\times10^{-15})(1.0\times10^5)}{5.0\times10^{-4}} = \frac{5.0\times10^{-10}}{5.0\times10^{-4}} = 1.0\times10^{-6}\;\mathrm{mol/(m^2{\cdot}s)}$$

The steady-state CO₂ flux is 1.0 μmol/(m²·s). For a 0.5 L bottle with $\approx$ 0.02 m² of wall area, that is roughly 1.7 mmol/day of lost carbonation. With an initial 10 mmol of dissolved CO₂, the bottle goes flat in about a week at room temperature even if sealed. Barrier coatings (EVOH, diamond-like carbon) that reduce $P$ by 10 to 100 times extend shelf life proportionally.

Selectivity

A membrane that separates two gases A and B has ideal selectivity:

(12.7) $$\alpha_{A/B} = \frac{P_A}{P_B} = \frac{D_A\,S_A}{D_B\,S_B}$$
High selectivity means the membrane passes gas A much more readily than gas B. This is the figure of merit for membrane gas separation: CO₂ removal from natural gas, O₂ enrichment from air, and H₂ purification all rely on maximizing $\alpha$. There is a fundamental Robeson upper-bound tradeoff: high $P_A$ tends to come with low $\alpha$.

12.5 Non-Fickian (Case II) Diffusion

Chapter 11 assumed Fickian diffusion: diffusion is fast compared to the chain-relaxation dynamics of the polymer. In glassy polymers, this assumption breaks down. Penetrant molecules must wait for chain segments to relax before they can jump, so the relaxation timescale $\tau_r$ competes with the diffusion timescale $\tau_D = L^2/D$.

The Deborah number for mass transfer quantifies this competition:

(12.8) $$\mathrm{De}_m = \frac{\tau_r}{\tau_D}$$
$\mathrm{De}_m \ll 1$: relaxation is fast; Fickian diffusion. The concentration profile is Gaussian (diffuse front). $\mathrm{De}_m \gg 1$: relaxation is slow; Case II transport. A sharp solvent front advances at constant velocity. The name comes from the linear (Case I) versus constant-velocity (Case II) regimes originally catalogued by Alfrey, Gurnee, and Lloyd.

Case II Transport

In Case II, penetrant is absorbed in a narrow swollen zone just behind the advancing front. The front position $x_{\rm front}$ grows linearly with time:

(12.9) $$x_{\rm front} = v \cdot t$$
$v$ [m/s] is the Case II front velocity, a material and penetrant-specific constant. Compare to Fickian penetration depth $\delta_D = 2\sqrt{Dt}$ which grows as $\sqrt{t}$. In Case II, a plot of penetration depth vs. time gives a straight line; in Fickian diffusion it gives a square-root curve. Experimentally distinguishing the two is therefore straightforward.

Figure 12.3: Concentration profiles for Fickian diffusion (left, blue) and Case II transport (right, red). Fickian diffusion produces a smooth, spreading erfc profile. Case II produces a sharp step: behind the front the polymer is fully swollen at concentration $C_0$; ahead it is dry. The front advances at constant velocity $v$ rather than the $\sqrt{t}$ rate of Fickian diffusion. This behavior is common in glassy polymers exposed to organic solvents.

Example 12.3

Case II front penetration in an epoxy adhesive

Methanol diffuses into a crosslinked epoxy via Case II transport with front velocity $v = 2.0\times10^{-7}$ m/s. A bonded joint requires the front to stay below 1.0 mm from the surface for at least 5.0 hours. Does it meet this requirement?

Solution

Convert time: $t = 5.0 \times 3600 = 18{,}000$ s. Apply Eq. 12.9:

$$x_{\rm front} = v\,t = (2.0\times10^{-7})(18{,}000) = 3.6\times10^{-3}\;\mathrm{m} = 3.6\;\mathrm{mm}$$

The front reaches 3.6 mm in 5 hours, far exceeding the 1.0 mm safety limit. The joint does not meet the requirement. To keep $x_{\rm front} \le 1.0$ mm, the maximum allowable time is $t_{\rm max} = 10^{-3}/(2.0\times10^{-7}) = 5000$ s $\approx$ 83 minutes. A coating or surface treatment that reduces $v$ by at least threefold would extend this to the required 5 hours.

Concept Summary

Crystalline regions in a polymer are impermeable to small-molecule penetrants. They reduce $D_{\rm eff}$ through two effects: reducing the amorphous fraction available for diffusion ($\phi_{\rm am}$) and forcing a tortuous path ($\tau > 1$). Biaxial orientation of lamellae, as in stretch-blown PET, maximizes $\tau$ in the transport direction, providing barrier enhancement beyond what crystallinity fraction alone predicts.

Concept Summary

The Arrhenius equation $D = D_0\exp(-E_a/RT)$ predicts that $\ln D$ is linear in $1/T$ with slope $-E_a/R$. A larger $E_a$ gives a steeper slope, meaning $D$ is more sensitive to temperature. For a given temperature change, the ratio $D_2/D_1 = \exp[(E_a/R)(1/T_1 - 1/T_2)]$. With $E_a = 40$ kJ/mol over a 50 K increase from 300 K to 350 K, $D$ increases by a factor of $e^{2.30} = 10$.

Concept Summary

Permeability $P = D \cdot S$ combines diffusivity (how fast) and solubility (how much). Two polymers with the same $P$ can have very different $D$ and $S$ values, with implications for selectivity between gases. The steady-state flux $J = P\Delta P/L$ is directly proportional to $P$ and $\Delta P$, and inversely proportional to thickness $L$. Concept Summary

Case II transport occurs in glassy polymers where chain segment relaxation ($\tau_r$) is much slower than diffusion ($\tau_D = L^2/D$), giving $\mathrm{De}_m \gg 1$. The experimental signature is mass uptake proportional to time $t$ (not $\sqrt{t}$) and a sharp, step-like concentration profile advancing at constant velocity. Above $T_g$, relaxation is fast and Fickian behavior is recovered. The transition between regimes passes through an intermediate anomalous (non-Fickian) regime near $\mathrm{De}_m \approx 1$.

Concept Summary

Effective barrier design exploits multiple mechanisms. Material selection (polymer class) is the most powerful lever, since $P$ varies by over ten orders of magnitude across polymer families. Within a class, maximizing crystallinity (tortuosity) provides moderate improvement. Chapter Summary

Crystallinity Effect

Crystalline lamellae are impermeable. They reduce $D_{\rm eff}$ by decreasing the amorphous volume fraction and increasing the tortuosity of the diffusion path. Oriented lamellae (drawn PET) maximize tortuosity in the transport direction.

Arrhenius Temperature Dependence

$D = D_0\exp(-E_a/RT)$. The ratio form $\ln(D_2/D_1) = (E_a/R)(1/T_1 - 1/T_2)$ is used for calculation. Larger $E_a$ means greater temperature sensitivity. Typical values: 20 to 80 kJ/mol for gases in polymers.

Free-Volume Theory

$D = A\exp(-B/v_f)$. Diffusion requires free-volume fluctuations large enough for the penetrant. At $T_g$, the temperature dependence of $v_f$ changes slope, causing a kink in the Arrhenius plot and a dramatic change in $D$.

Permeability

$P = D \cdot S$ combines diffusivity (kinetic) and solubility (thermodynamic). Flux: $J = P\,\Delta P/L$. In a multilayer film, layers act as resistances in series; the lowest-$P$ layer dominates. The Robeson upper bound limits $P$ vs. selectivity tradeoffs.

Selectivity

$\alpha_{A/B} = P_A/P_B = D_A S_A/(D_B S_B)$. High selectivity is required for membrane separations. Rubbery polymers tend to be solubility-selective; glassy polymers tend to be diffusivity-selective (small molecules pass preferentially).

Case II Transport

In glassy polymers with $\mathrm{De}_m = \tau_r/\tau_D \gg 1$, a sharp front advances at $x_{\rm front} = v\,t$ (linear in time, not $\sqrt{t}$). Signature: mass uptake linear in $t$; step-like concentration profile. Occurs in organic solvents swelling glassy polymers.

Key Equations
  • Arrhenius: $D = D_0\exp(-E_a/RT)$
  • Two-T form: $\ln(D_2/D_1) = (E_a/R)(1/T_1 - 1/T_2)$
  • Free volume: $D = A\exp(-B/v_f)$
  • Permeability: $P = D\cdot S$
  • Flux: $J = P\,\Delta P/L$
  • Selectivity: $\alpha_{A/B} = P_A/P_B$
  • Diff. timescale: $\tau_D = L^2/D$
  • Case II front: $x_{\rm front} = v\,t$
